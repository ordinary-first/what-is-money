# Abstract

Economics defines money through three functions — medium of exchange, store of value, unit of account — and builds its theories upon that definition as a constant. This works in ordinary times, but in moments of crisis, what was treated as a constant reveals itself to be a variable. The theorists who have directly asked about the nature of money — Menger, Knapp, Graeber, Minsky, Polanyi — have remained unreconciled with one another for over 150 years, each advancing their own layer. This theory holds that this division exists not because they were wrong, but because each was touching a different part of the same phenomenon called money. The moment money is defined not as **an object with properties but as an emergent phenomenon** in human collectives, the existing insights are not discarded but repositioned within a single framework. This is the starting point of the theory.

The foundational premise of this theory is as follows. **Money is a ledger. That is, the essence of money is a record of debt.** This thesis transcends both metallism, which locates the essence of money in the intrinsic value of a material, and chartalism, which locates it in the legal enforceability of the state. The rai stones of Yap Island sank into the sea a hundred years ago, never recovered, yet continue to function as money to this day — what operates in the absence of the physical medium is only the community's shared record. The fact that inter-state gold transfers in the vault of the New York Federal Reserve are effected not by moving bullion but by changing ledger tags reveals that even the gold standard was essentially a ledger system. The fact that the Swiss dinar, legally abolished by Saddam Hussein, maintained its value in Kurdistan shows that state legal coercion is not a necessary condition for monetary operation. Whether the medium is shells, gold, paper, or blockchain, money operates not through the properties of the medium but through a socially shared and maintained record.

Upon this premise, the theory erects two core claims. **First, the value of money is determined not by the properties of the medium but by the collective sum of the costs actually paid by current holders to acquire that money — their sunk cost.** These costs accumulate across three dimensions: economic, social, and cognitive. **Second, there exists an irresolvable structural conflict between the force that preserves accumulated sunk cost (preservation force) and the force that dilutes it at the collective level (coordination force), and this conflict drives the entire life cycle of money — adoption, stability, crisis, collapse, and transition.**

These two principles operate simultaneously across **seven layers**. The primate instinct for fairness and human ultra-sociality form the evolutionary-biological foundation for monetary emergence in Layer 0 (the human condition); arbitrary selection is converted into necessity through sunk cost accumulation in Layer 1 (adoption); medium technology constrains the channels through which conflict manifests in Layer 2 (ledger authority); credit and interest rates pre-claim future USC in the present in Layer 3 (temporal folding); the divergence between market price and USC is self-referentially amplified in Layer 4 (financial self-proliferation); multiple currencies coexist by differentiating into niches in Layer 5 (ecological competition); and the four-phase cycle repeats self-similarly at the scale of nations, empires, and civilizations in Layer 6 (macro-circulation). The layers are genetically sequential yet structurally simultaneous.

This framework does not discard the insights of monetary theorists who have remained unreconciled for 150 years. Menger's saleability is positioned at Layer 1, Graeber's debt-protocol at Layer 2, Mises's regression theorem at Layer 3, Minsky's financial instability hypothesis at Layer 4, and Polanyi's double movement at Layers 5–6. Each theorist was not wrong — they captured different layers of the same phenomenon. This theory repositions these partial insights within a single framework.

The universality of this framework is supported by empirical verification spanning 5 civilizations, 4 continents, and 5,000 years. The non-linearity by which a 96% reduction in silver content in the Roman denarius produced a 200-fold rise in prices is explained by the sequential collapse of triple USC. The fact that MVRV (market price ÷ USC) hit Bitcoin's cycle peaks across four cycles without false positives is the quantitative expression of the Minsky cycle. Cowries, the denarius, the pound, the dollar, and Bitcoin are all described in one language.

Therefore, the utility of this theory lies not in explaining the past. **What repeats is not the event but the mechanism.** Just as the law of pendulum motion repeats yet each swing traces a different trajectory, the preservation-coordination conflict manifests in a different form each time yet follows the same structure. Decoding this structure is the only way to gauge the current monetary phase and the phase that follows.

# Section 1: Introduction

## 1.1 The Skipped Question: Why Does Economics Not Ask About the Nature of Money?

Economics defines money. Medium of exchange, store of value, unit of account. These three functions constitute the definition of money, and economics places this definition as a **constant** upon which it builds its theories. The money supply (M), the interest rate (r), and the exchange rate (e) are treated as variables, but the question "what is money?" itself is processed as a definition and bypassed. The assumption is that what changes is the quantity of money, not its essence.

This assumption works in ordinary times. GDP is calculated in "real" terms, inflation is treated as a function of monetary policy, and money remains in the background as a transparent medium of economic activity. Just as physics proceeds by treating gravity as a constant, economics fixes the essence of money and analyzes production, consumption, distribution, and growth upon that fixed foundation.

But when a crisis arrives, what was treated as a constant reveals itself to be a variable.

In 2019, bank-deposit dollars in Lebanon began trading at an 80% discount to physical dollars. The same "dollar" existed at four different prices. For the definition of "medium of exchange" to hold, one dollar must be one dollar anywhere — but it was not. In Argentina, real estate is transacted in terms of "metric tons of soybeans." Because the peso has lost its function as a "store of value," soybeans have taken the place of money. In May 2022, Terra-Luna — with a market capitalization of $40 billion — collapsed to zero in 72 hours. At the same time, Bitcoin fell 77% but survived. Neither was a "medium of exchange" nor a "store of value" in any conventional sense, yet one died and the other survived. The three-function definition cannot explain this difference.

**When what has been treated as a definitional constant begins to waver, the entire analytical framework built upon it loses its foundation.** Friedman's Quantity Theory of Money (MV = PY) states that changes in M (money supply) are proportional to changes in P (price level), but when the silver content per unit of the Roman denarius fell by a factor of 25, prices rose by a factor of 200. The relationship is nonlinear. To explain this discrepancy, one needs a deeper understanding of "what money is," but because economics skipped that question, it lacks the framework to provide an answer.

### Those Who Posed the Skipped Question — and Their Limits

Of course, there have been theorists who directly asked about the nature of money. Yet among them exists a schism that has not been resolved in over 150 years.

**A schism over origins.** In 1892, Carl Menger argued that money emerged spontaneously from the market — that the most "saleable" commodity naturally came to be selected as the medium of exchange. Knapp (1905) countered that money is whatever the state declares it to be. In 2011, anthropologist Graeber demonstrated with thousands of pieces of evidence that debt existed before money, fundamentally negating Menger's barter narrative. All three positions have counter-examples, and none has definitively refuted the others.

**A schism over instability.** Friedman argued that monetary systems are inherently stable and that instability arises from policy failure. Minsky argued the opposite — "stability breeds instability." During stable periods, risk-taking naturally increases, transitioning from hedge to speculative to Ponzi financing; this occurs from within the system itself, without any external shock. The 2008 financial crisis appeared to dramatically vindicate Minsky, but the Friedmanite school continues to maintain the policy-failure explanation.

**A schism over the relationship between money and society.** Liberal economics views money as a neutral tool of the market. Polanyi (1944) characterized money as a "fictitious commodity" and argued that treating it as a pure market commodity destroys society, to which society instinctively responds (the double movement). Each time quantitative easing, cryptocurrency, or CBDCs appear, this debate recurs in a new form.

The reason this schism remains unresolved is that **each theorist was capturing different layers of the same phenomenon called money.** Menger was looking at why money originates; Graeber at how it becomes institutionalized; Minsky at why it becomes unstable; Polanyi at how it interacts with society. They were touching different parts of the elephant. And each theory falls silent on phenomena outside its own layer — Menger's saleability cannot explain the value gap between Bitcoin and its forks; Minsky's financial instability cannot explain why Bitcoin survived four crashes of 80% or more; and Polanyi's double movement cannot explain why twenty-first-century resistance takes the form of cryptocurrency rather than the welfare state.

### Why the Whole Has Not Been Visible

The fact that mainstream economics skipped the question of money's essence, and the fact that monetary theorists each saw only their own layer — these two problems share the same common cause. **They looked at money as an object.** When viewed as an object, money is either a thing with properties, an institution with structure, or a policy instrument with effects. Menger studied the properties of gold, Friedman studied the variable called the money supply, and Minsky studied the dynamics of financial structure. Each was an explanation specific to a particular kind of money and a particular era. An object can be decomposed, and the decomposed pieces can each be studied separately. That is why the schism developed and unification did not occur.

This theory looks at money not as an object but as a **phenomenon.**[^oh-phenomenon] Money is a phenomenon that appears when Homo sapiens forms groups exceeding Dunbar's number (~150 individuals). Just as ant colonies create optimal paths without any design, humans acting according to their own self-interested motives unintentionally create the complex system called money. The essence of money therefore resides not in the physical properties of gold or the institutional structure of the dollar, but in **the instincts of Homo sapiens and the dynamics inherent in human society.** The attachment to sunk costs is an evolutionary biological instinct at the primate level; the tension between preservation force and coordination force is a dynamic inherent in any collective facing a public goods problem. These are not limited to any particular currency or era.

[^oh-phenomenon]: Taemin Oh characterized Bitcoin as "a total phenomenon with no field left unaffected," arguing for an integrated analytical framework spanning humanities, history, philosophy, and economics. Oh Taemin, *The Great Bitcoin* (Geoin's Garden, 2024); *A Future Without Bitcoin Is Impossible* (Geoin's Garden, 2025). This theory extends the "total phenomenon" perspective beyond Bitcoin to all moneys across all eras.

When viewed as a phenomenon, the schism dissolves. What Menger saw is the adoption layer (Layer 1) of this phenomenon; what Graeber saw is the institutionalization layer (Layer 2); what Minsky saw is the self-referential amplification layer (Layer 4); and what Polanyi saw is the ecological competition and macro-cyclical layers (Layers 5–6). They were not wrong — they saw parts. This theory places these parts within a single framework.

This shift in perspective grants the theory two advantages. **First, universality.** Whether the currency is gold coinage, paper money, or cryptocurrency, the same dynamics operate — because the species using it is the same. **Second, predictive power.** Once the essence of money is understood as the structural dynamics of human society, it becomes possible to make predictions about monetary phenomena that have not yet occurred.

## 1.2 Core Claims

This theory rests on two core claims.

**First, what determines the value of money is not the properties of the medium but the accumulated sunk cost.** Gold's scarcity, state declaration, money supply — these are conditions of monetary value, not its determinants. The determinant is the sum total of costs that holders actually paid to acquire the medium. Even when the medium changes, the sunk cost transfers; money without sunk cost collapses.

Bitcoin and Bitcoin Cash originated from identical code. Their technical properties are nearly the same. Yet their values differ by a factor of hundreds. Neither Menger's saleability, nor Knapp's state declaration, nor Friedman's money supply can explain this gap. Only one thing can — Bitcoin has accumulated sunk cost built up over 15 years, and Bitcoin Cash does not. Technology can be copied; sunk cost cannot.[^oh-btc-bch]

[^oh-btc-bch]: Taemin Oh explains the value gap between Bitcoin and its hard forks through the concept of sunk cost that cannot be copied, arguing why Bitcoin's absolute value differs fundamentally from fork coins. Oh Taemin, *The Great Bitcoin* (Geoin's Garden, 2024).

**Second, there exists an irresolvable structural conflict between the force that preserves individual sunk cost (preservation force) and the force that dilutes it at the collective level (coordination force), and this conflict drives the entire dynamics of money.** Adoption, stability, crisis, collapse, transition — the entire life cycle of money is an expression of this conflict.

Preservation force is the force by which individuals seek to protect their own sunk cost — the Argentine farmer who refuses to sell soybeans, the Japanese corporation that hoards cash, the Bitcoin holder who refuses to sell. Coordination force is the force that dilutes individual accumulation at the collective level — taxation, inflation, government bonds. These two forces are structurally incompatible: if sunk cost is completely preserved (zero inflation, fixed money supply), the collective loses its means of responding to problems at the collective level; if coordination force is maximized without limit (unlimited money printing), individual sunk cost is obliterated. No institutional design can resolve this tension — it can only be managed.

### Structural Implications Derived from the Core Claims

Accepting these two claims entails three structural implications. These are not separate propositions but logical corollaries of the core claims.

**Emergence.** Money is not designed but arises naturally. When each individual acts according to the self-interested motive of preserving their own sunk cost, the aggregation of millions of actions produces, without intention, a collective consensus that "this is money." It is the same structure by which an ant colony creates an optimal path without design. However, because humans are capable of recursive prediction — "everyone believes the price of gold will rise → so they buy gold → the price of gold actually rises" — the emergence of money encompasses both self-reinforcing acceleration and self-destructive collapse.

**Multilayered operation.** The preservation-coordination conflict does not operate on a single dimension. Adoption (Layer 1), ledger authority (Layer 2), temporal folding (Layer 3), self-referential amplification (Layer 4), ecological competition (Layer 5), macro-cyclicality (Layer 6) — the same conflict operates simultaneously across multiple layers. The reason existing theorists each captured only a different layer is precisely this multilayered nature — Menger was looking at Layer 1, Minsky at Layer 4, and Polanyi at Layers 5–6.

**Cyclicality.** Because the preservation-coordination conflict is irresolvable, it manifests cyclically through time: preservation dominance → coordination infiltration → coordination dominance → collapse/reset. These four phases are observed in the Roman denarius (254 years), the Argentine peso (recurring crises over decades), and 29 hyperinflationary episodes (Bernholz). What recurs is the mechanism, not the event — just as the law of the pendulum's motion repeats but the specific trajectory of each swing differs. This distinction is the key to differentiating between the historicism Popper criticized (predicting the repetition of events) and this theory (describing the repetition of mechanisms). This cycle operates self-similarly at all scales — national (years to decades), imperial (decades to centuries), and civilizational (centuries to millennia) — and can also proceed in reverse (regression). The detailed dynamics are treated in Layer 6.

## 1.3 Principles of the Theory

### Principle 1: Neutral Descriptive Dynamics — Exclusion of Value Judgments

This theory does not prescribe what money "ought to be." It describes only how money "operates."

It is true that the rigidity of the gold standard exacerbated the Great Depression, but that does not make the gold standard "bad." It is true that quantitative easing mitigated the immediate fallout of the 2008 financial crisis, but that does not make quantitative easing "good." In the terms of this theory, both the extreme of preservation force (the rigidity of the gold standard) and the extreme of coordination force (hyperinflation) are simply states within the dynamics of money.

This neutrality is a deliberate choice. Nearly all discourse about money is contaminated by value judgments. "Inflation is bad," "central banks are necessary," "Bitcoin is a speculative asset" — such preconceptions obstruct objective observation of the phenomenon. Just as a physicist does not say that gravity is "good" or "bad" when describing it, this theory describes the dynamics of money in a neutral manner.

### Principle 2: Universality — Applicable to All Currencies and All Eras

A valid theory of money must not be applicable only to a particular era or particular type of currency. It must apply equally to the stone money (rai) of Yap Island, to the Roman denarius, to the jiaozi of the Song Dynasty, to the cowrie shells of West Africa, to the modern U.S. dollar, and to Bitcoin.

This theory pursues that universality, and through research has verified that the core structure of the theory (7 layers) holds without exception across 5 civilizations (Mesopotamia, China, West Africa, Mesoamerica, the Pacific), 4 continents, and 5,000 years. The basis for this universality is the synchronization verification across 11 empire-currency pairs and the empirical research across 3 phases and 10 tasks.

### Principle 3: Integration — Repositioning Rather Than Discarding Existing Theory

This theory does not discard the insights of Menger, Graeber, Minsky, Polanyi, Friedman, Mises, Keynes, Schelling, or Ferguson. What each theorist captured is truth — but partial truth. ## 2.1 Money Is a Social Accounting Ledger

The essence of money is not a thing but **the data of a social accounting ledger**.[^oh-ledger-theory]

[^oh-ledger-theory]: Taemin Oh advances the thesis that "money is not matter but a ledger," arguing that humanity substituted physical materials such as gold for ledger records solely due to the absence of the requisite technology. "The ideal ledger is the ideal money." Oh Taemin, EBS Class e, *Bitcoin That Only I Don't Know*, Episode 2: "Money Is a Ledger" (2022); *The Great Bitcoin* (Geoin's Garden, 2024), Chapter 23.

Money is **social** not because a ledger is shared among many people, but because the phenomenon of money itself emerges only at the moment when humans form groups that exceed Dunbar's number (~150 individuals) — a matter treated in detail in Layer 0. In groups of 150 or fewer, the exchange of surplus and the tracking of obligations can be managed through direct relationships and reputation alone. Beyond that threshold, individual memory is no longer capable of tracking "who contributed how much, and who is entitled to claim how much." The externalization of that tracking is the ledger, and the abstract unit recorded in the ledger is money. Money is a product of social exchange and cannot exist outside of society.

Money is an **accounting** ledger because this record takes the form of numbers rather than narrative. A ledger does not contain stories. It records only balances and transfers of an abstract unit — who holds how much, and how much has moved from whom to whom. These numbers do not correspond to physical assets in any fixed way. The only moment at which numbers meet the physical world is the instant of exchange: when someone receives those numbers and voluntarily decides to hand over goods in return.

Money is an accounting **ledger** because this information must be recorded and preserved in some form. In Yap, the collective memory of the community served as the ledger; in Sumer, clay tablets; in Florence, double-entry account books; in the modern era, the electronic databases of banks; in Bitcoin, the blockchain. The form differs, but the function is the same. And when the record disappears, money disappears with it.

When the form of the ledger changes, a question of authority arises — who administers the ledger: who may write, who may read, who may alter, and who may access the records. Gold is heavy and costly to verify, so authority concentrates in whoever is entrusted with its custody and verification. Paper is easy to counterfeit, making a monopoly on issuance inevitable. Electronic ledgers have zero issuance cost, so restraint depends entirely on will — and historically, that will has always failed. This allocation of authority — monopolized by Sumerian priests, seized by Roman emperors, abused by medieval sovereigns, institutionalized by modern states, and distributed by Bitcoin — is the central variable in monetary dynamics and is treated in detail in Layer 2 (Ledger Authority).

There are three received ideas that this premise rejects.

### Money Is Not a Thing

Gold coins are things. Banknotes are things. Coins are things. The intuition that value is intrinsic to these objects is natural — and wrong.

Milton Friedman opens the first chapter of *Money Mischief* (1992) with the stone money of Yap Island.[^friedman-yap] The inhabitants of Yap in Micronesia used enormous limestone discs — rai — as money. Quarried on the island of Palau, 400 kilometers away, they were transported by canoe. Some hundred years ago, one rai was lost to a storm during transport and sank to the bottom of the sea. No one retrieved it. **Yet that sunken rai continued to circulate as money on Yap Island.**

[^friedman-yap]: Milton Friedman, *Money Mischief: Episodes in Monetary History* (Harcourt, 1992), Ch. 1, "The Island of Stone Money."

If money were a thing, how could a stone at the bottom of the sea — unreachable and untouchable — function as money? What mattered to the people of Yap was not the stone itself but **the information "that rai belongs to so-and-so."** Every member of the village remembered that information, and when ownership changed, the memory was updated. The stone was nothing more than a physical anchor for the information. Whether the anchor lay on the ocean floor or in the village square, money functioned so long as the information lived.

Friedman compares this to the Federal Reserve Bank of New York's gold holdings. When France requested a transfer of gold from the United States, what actually occurred was that gold bars were moved from one compartment to another in the New York vault. The gold did not move. What changed was the entry in the ledger — from "this gold belongs to the United States" to "this gold belongs to France." This is essentially identical to what the people of Yap did when they updated their collective memory of who owned the sunken rai.

### Money Does Not Operate Through Guarantee

"It may not be a thing, but surely it requires state guarantee for money to function?" The Iraqi Swiss dinar answers this question.

After the Gulf War of 1990, Saddam Hussein's regime issued a new currency (the Saddam dinar) and demonetized the old one (the Swiss dinar). Yet in the politically isolated Kurdish region, the old currency continued to circulate. Kurdistan had no printing plates, no central bank, no government to back the currency. Its legal enforceability was zero.

The outcome was the inverse of what economics textbooks would predict. **The Swiss dinar — guaranteed by no one — held its value, while the Saddam dinar — issued and enforced by the state — depreciated at more than 30 percent per year.** When a unified currency was introduced in 2003, one Swiss dinar exchanged for 150 new dinars. The supply of Swiss dinars was physically fixed — no additional issuance was possible, since there were no printing plates. The Saddam dinar was printed by the regime without limit. It was not state guarantee that made money function; it was the supply structure that sustained people's voluntary willingness to exchange.

### Money Is Not Energy

There is a common practice of comparing money to energy. But energy can do work without society — it generates heat, moves objects, drives chemical reactions. Money can do nothing by itself. Stranded on a deserted island with a billion dollars, one can accomplish nothing with it; it makes worse kindling than a lighter. Money does "work" only when it is connected to society — only when there exists someone who will receive those numbers and hand over goods in return. Energy is a physical substance; money is a social relation.

### The Remaining Question

If money is neither a thing, nor something that operates through guarantee, nor something that can accomplish anything on its own like energy — if it is, rather, an abstract number in a social ledger — one question remains. Why do people surrender real goods and labor in order to obtain this number?

This is precisely the question that Permeating Principle 1 of this theory — **Collective Sunk Cost** — answers.

## 2.2 Permeating Principle 1: Collective Sunk Cost

### 2.2.1 Definition

> **"Trust in money derives from the collective accumulation of costs (economic, social, and cognitive) that current holders paid to obtain that money. The initial choice is arbitrary, but the accumulation of sunk cost transforms that contingency into necessity."**[^oh-usc]

[^oh-usc]: Taemin Oh proposed the central insight that Bitcoin's value comes not from its technology but from the accumulation of costs invested to obtain it, and treats "cost and trust" as a central theme. This insight became the seed of this theory's Permeating Principle 1 (Collective Sunk Cost). Oh Taemin, *21 Million* (Geoin's Garden, 2025).

In economics, a sunk cost is a cost already paid that cannot be recovered, and it is conventionally regarded as something that should be ignored in rational decision-making. However, humans do not ignore sunk costs. As behavioral economics has repeatedly shown, humans are attached to costs already incurred, and this attachment fixes their choices. This theory argues that this "irrationality" is the core mechanism of money.

Whatever the object — whether a shell, gold, paper, or an electronic record — once people pay real costs to obtain it, the collective accumulation of those costs creates the collective conviction that "this has value." This conviction does not come from the physical properties of the object but from the total sum of costs that people have paid for it. The reason gold has value is not that gold glitters, but that over thousands of years hundreds of millions of people have paid enormous costs to obtain it.

This may appear to be circular reasoning: "costs are paid because gold is expensive, and gold is expensive because costs have been paid." But this is not a defect — it is the core. Money is fundamentally a self-referential phenomenon, and this self-referentiality is precisely the mechanism of emergence. The pheromone trail of ants has the same self-referential structure: "the trail is strong because ants follow it, and ants follow it because the trail is strong."

What matters is not how this cycle begins (the initial start is arbitrary — treated in Layer 1) but how, once begun, it accumulates in a self-reinforcing manner.

### 2.2.2 The Triple Structure of Sunk Cost

The sunk cost accumulated in money is not singular but is composed of three dimensions. The interpretation of this triple structure is based on Graeber's insights.

**Economic sunk cost**: Labor and resources invested to obtain money. The physical labor to mine gold, the electricity and hardware to mine Bitcoin, the goods and services provided to earn dollars. This is the most direct and measurable form of sunk cost.

**Social sunk cost**: The relational and reputational costs invested in order to participate in the monetary system. The reputation built through credit transactions, the networks constructed to participate in the financial system, the alternative relationships foregone by belonging to a particular monetary community. The "debt relationships" that Graeber emphasizes are the core form of this social sunk cost.

**Cognitive sunk cost**: The psychological costs invested in belief and cultural internalization of money. The cognitive investment made in internalizing the belief that "the dollar is safe," the costs of learning and propagating the cultural narrative that "gold is eternal." This is the least visible but potentially the most powerful form of sunk cost. In hyperinflation, the very last thing people are able to relinquish is the cognitive sunk cost invested in their own national currency.

### 2.2.3 USC Dilution and the Nonlinear Loss of Monetary Trust

Sunk cost accumulates; it does not disappear. The labor invested in mining gold, the goods and services exchanged for denarii, the social relationships built within a particular monetary system and the cognitive beliefs internalized within it — these exist permanently as irrecoverable sunk costs from the moment they are paid. What happens when money fails is not the destruction of this sunk cost but the dilution of USC. When new money is issued in large quantities at low cost, the total money supply increases, the denominator of the USC formula grows, and USC falls. The sunk cost invested by existing holders remains unchanged, but the density of trust it backs per unit of currency grows progressively thinner.

The mechanism by which this dilution leads to a nonlinear loss of monetary trust is demonstrated by the Roman denarius. Over the 3rd century, the silver content of the denarius fell approximately 96% over 254 years (from approximately 3.9 g of pure silver to approximately 0.15 g). If the relationship were simply proportional, prices should have risen 25-fold. In reality, prices rose by more than 200-fold.

This eightfold discrepancy is explained by the triple structure of sunk cost. The reduction in silver content directly dilutes the economic dimension of USC. However, once USC falls below a certain level, the social sunk cost that people have accumulated within that monetary system (trade practices, credit networks) and the cognitive sunk cost ("this currency is trustworthy" as a collective belief) can no longer sustain trust in the currency. The sunk cost itself still exists, but the USC of the currency unit it backs has become too low. People invested relationships and reputations in "the practice of transacting in denarii," but once the currency that is the object of that practice loses its value, that social capital is neutralized with it. This is the mechanism by which the dilution of economic USC propagates in cascade through the social and cognitive dimensions — and it explains the gap between 25-fold and 200-fold.

This pattern is identically observed in modern hyperinflationary episodes — the Weimar Republic, Zimbabwe, Venezuela. The arithmetic effect of money supply growth alone cannot explain the speed and extremity of hyperinflation. What is decisive is the moment when USC falls below the threshold and the three dimensions of sunk cost can no longer sustain monetary trust — the moment when all three dimensions are simultaneously neutralized.

### 2.2.4 The Economic Substantiality of Sunk Cost

Collective sunk cost is not a psychological concept or a subjective valuation. It is the aggregate of real economic costs actually incurred by participants, and these costs are physically measurable economic quantities.

**Bitcoin: the case that most clearly demonstrates the physical reality of USC.**

Mining Bitcoin requires electricity, ASIC hardware, cooling systems, and the construction of facilities. These are not abstract concepts — they are physical costs recorded on electricity meters and in accounting ledgers. The cumulative mining cost of the Bitcoin network amounts to tens of billions of dollars, and this represents real resources consumed, not "trust" or "consensus." Moreover, because every Bitcoin transaction is recorded on the blockchain, one can observe in real time a near-exact approximation of USC — the Realized Cap (the sum of each UTXO's price at the time of its last movement). For the first time in history, the sunk cost of a currency has become transparently measurable.

**BTC vs. BCH: proof that "technology can be copied, sunk cost cannot."**

In 2017, Bitcoin Cash (BCH) was launched by copying Bitcoin's code outright (a fork). Its technical properties are nearly identical, and in certain technical respects — with a larger block size — it was even "superior." By Menger's saleability criterion, the values of the two currencies should be similar, since their physical properties are similar. In reality, BTC maintains a value more than 100 times that of BCH. Only one thing can explain this — **BTC carries sunk cost accumulated since 2009, and BCH does not.** The code was copied, but the electricity, hardware, time, social networks, and cognitive trust invested over eight years were not. This is the cleanest natural experiment for this theory's core claim — "the value of money is determined not by the properties of the medium but by the accumulated sunk cost."

**Pi Coin: opportunity cost is also reflected in USC.**

The economic substantiality of sunk cost operates even when the investment of physical resources is minimal. Mining Pi Coin (Pi Network) consists of pressing a button in a smartphone app — with almost no physical resource investment. However, time is invested. Even if a Korean participant and a Bangladeshi participant invest the same amount of time, the opportunity cost of that time is dramatically different — when the Korean hourly wage is approximately 10,000 won, the Bangladeshi hourly wage is approximately 500 won. USC directly reflects this difference in actual opportunity cost. Since the acquisition cost is the real economic cost (including opportunity cost), USC necessarily reflects real economic conditions.

This matters because of the decisive difference from existing approaches that explain trust in money through "collective illusion," "social consensus," or "psychological bias." In this theory, the sunk cost that underlies monetary value has economic substance — to the question "why do people believe this has value?", this theory answers with "cost" rather than "belief."

### 2.2.5 The Spectrum of USC Measurement: Transparency and Opacity

However, "having economic substance" and "being precisely measurable" are different things. The measurability of USC varies dramatically depending on the type of medium.

**Bitcoin: fully transparent.** All transactions are recorded on the blockchain. Summing the price at the time of each UTXO's last movement yields the Realized Cap — that is, the total acquisition cost of current holders — in real time. For the first time in history, the USC of a currency exists in a form that anyone can verify without a third party. Because MVRV (market price ÷ USC) can be observed in real time, it is possible to detect in advance when the divergence between market price and USC is approaching a critical threshold (4 out of 4 cycle peak calls matched).

**Gold: highly estimable.** The acquisition cost of gold is estimated by mining cost (All-In Sustaining Cost for all gold mines worldwide, approximately $1,200–1,400/oz as of 2024). Precise aggregation is impossible, but reasonable estimation based on industry data is feasible. For historical gold, indirect estimation is possible from silver content (of the denarius), mining difficulty, and labor inputs.

**Fiat currency: not directly measurable, only indirectly estimable.** This is not simply a matter of insufficient data — it is an impossibility owing to **structural reasons.**

Structural reasons why the USC of fiat currency is not directly measurable:

First, **acquisition pathways are heterogeneous.** Even for the same 10,000-won note, the sunk cost is fundamentally different depending on how it was acquired. The USC of 10,000 won earned through 8 hours of labor is the opportunity cost of those 8 hours. The USC of 10,000 won generated through a bank loan is nearly zero at the time of creation (a commercial bank simply entering a number in a ledger). The USC of base money created by a central bank through quantitative easing is literally zero (generated with a single keystroke). These three varieties of 10,000 won circulate indistinguishably mixed together, making it impossible to aggregate USC.

Second, **the credit multiplier structurally dilutes USC.** Under fractional reserve banking, a deposit of 100 creates, through a loan of 90, a money supply of 190. The original depositor's sunk cost is 100, but the money in circulation is 190, so USC is diluted to 100/190 ≈ 0.53. As this process passes through multiple stages, the gap between "the sunk cost actually invested" and "the money supply in circulation" grows progressively wider. To accurately measure this gap, one would need to trace every chain of credit creation — which, in the modern financial system, is practically impossible.

Third, **derivatives and shadow finance add an untraceable layer.** Multilayering such as MBS→CDO→CDS renders the relationship between original sunk cost and market valuation opaque. That the 2008 financial crisis produced a state in which "no one knows what the actual USC is" was precisely the result of this multilayering.

**Indirect estimation is possible.** Even if direct measurement is impossible, proxies for estimating USC indirectly do exist:
- **M2/GDP ratio dynamics**: How much the money supply (M2) has expanded relative to the real economy (GDP). A sharp rise in this ratio is an indirect signal that USC dilution is accelerating.
- **Real interest rate**: Nominal interest rate minus inflation. A negative real interest rate indicates that "holders of money are not receiving preservation force compensation" — a symptom of the forced dilution of USC by coordination force.
- **Silver content of the denarius**: For historical currencies, changes in physical composition provide a direct tracking measure for USC dilution (silver content change from 96% to 0.5% over AD 1–254).

**The immeasurability itself is theoretically important.**

The opacity of fiat currency USC is not merely a technical limitation. This opacity is **the structural condition that makes the crises of Layer 4 (self-referential amplification) possible.** When USC can be observed in real time (Bitcoin's MVRV), market participants can detect and adjust when the divergence between market price and USC is approaching a danger level. When USC is opaque (fiat currency + multi-layered derivatives), no one can know the system's actual health, and crises appear to arrive "suddenly." In the 2008 financial crisis, the fact that the actual USC of subprime mortgages (the capacity to repay loans) became completely invisible as it passed through MBS→CDO→CDS was the dramatic realization of this mechanism.

**The spectrum of measurability:**

```
Fully Transparent ←────────────────────────→ Fully Opaque

Bitcoin (Realized Cap) → Gold (mining cost) → Bank deposits (partial est.) → Derivatives (untraceable)
USC precisely observable    Industry data est.    M2/GDP proxy only             Actual USC unknown
Crisis detectable in advance  Post-hoc estimable   Crisis detected late          Crisis arrives "suddenly"
```

This spectrum is directly connected to the evolution of medium technology in Layer 2 (ledger authority). The more transparent the ledger, the easier it is to measure USC; the more opaque, the harder. The essential change that Bitcoin's blockchain brought about is "making USC transparent for the first time" — whereas USC depended on estimates for gold, paper money, and electronic ledgers, in blockchain it becomes observable data. This is the reason it represents not merely a technical improvement but a **change in the epistemological status of money.**

### 2.2.6 Estimating USC in Fiat Currency: A System of Proxy Indicators

It is impossible to produce a single number for fiat currency USC in the way that Bitcoin's Realized Cap does. However, given that fiat currency accounts for the vast majority of the global economy, it is insufficient to simply declare that "measurement is difficult." By combining multiple proxies, one can track the **direction and velocity** of USC — whether it is accumulating or being diluted, and at what rate. The key is reading trends, not absolute values.

**① Aggregate level — M2/GDP ratio**

This shows how much the money supply (M2) has expanded relative to real economic activity (GDP). If this ratio is stable, it means that money issuance is proportional to real economic growth — USC dilution is limited. If the ratio rises sharply, the money supply is growing without real economic growth — USC is being diluted. In the United States, the M2/GDP ratio surged approximately 25% within 18 months in response to COVID in 2020; historically, without exception, inflation has followed a surge of this magnitude.

**② Price level — real interest rate**

The nominal interest rate minus inflation. A positive real interest rate indicates that holders of money are being compensated for the temporal dilution of USC — the preservation force is operative. A negative real interest rate indicates that the purchasing power of holders is diminishing over time — the coordination force is overwhelming the preservation force and forcibly diluting USC. During 2020–2022, the U.S. real interest rate reached -4% to -6%, meaning that holders of money were having their purchasing power involuntarily transferred at a rate of 4–6% per year. In the USC framework, a negative real interest rate is the forced absence of preservation force compensation by the coordination force; if this condition persists, holders migrate to alternatives (gold, real estate, Bitcoin) — this is the trigger condition for inter-niche capital flows treated in Layer 5 (ecological competition).

**③ Credit structure — debt/GDP ratio**

The mechanism by which credit creation structurally dilutes USC is treated in detail in Layer 3. The debt/GDP ratio is a proxy for "how much money has been created through credit relative to the real economy." The higher the ratio rises, the thinner the USC of the monetary system becomes as a signal. U.S. federal debt/GDP: 31% in 1980 → 55% in 2000 → 122% in 2024. Total debt/GDP, including private debt, exceeds 350%. This trend itself is a 40-year trajectory of expansion in the "advance appropriation of future sunk cost" (Layer 3).

**④ International comparison — Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) vs. market exchange rate**

The divergence between PPP exchange rates and market exchange rates reflects differences in the real USC of two countries' currencies. The fact that the same Big Mac costs $5.69 in the United States and $2.42 in India is because the labor and resource input costs (USC) of the two economies differ. The very existence of this divergence is indirect evidence for Core Claim 1 — if monetary value were determined by the properties of the medium, the same dollar would have the same purchasing power everywhere. In reality, purchasing power differs because the underlying economic sunk cost (labor costs, production costs, infrastructure investment) differs.

**⑤ Behavioral indicators — dollarization and capital flows**

The proportion of a country's population holding and using a foreign currency instead of its own (the dollarization ratio) is the holders' **collective vote** on the USC of the domestic currency. The dollarization ratio in Argentina exceeding 60%, the sharp rise in foreign-currency deposit ratios in Turkey, the separation of lollars from physical dollars in Lebanon — these are behavioral evidence that the USC of the domestic currency has fallen below holders' threshold. They also serve as real-time gauges of the Gresham-Thiers inversion treated in Layer 5.

Capital outflow data is relevant in the same context. As Rajan noted, capital inflows are the most powerful leading indicator of crisis; in the USC framework, capital inflows are reinterpreted as a process in which external USC dilutes internal USC — the inter-civilizational Cantillon effect (Layer 3).

**⑥ Historical currencies — changes in physical composition**

In the era of metallic currency, physical analysis enables direct tracking of USC. The silver content of the Roman denarius (AD 1–254: 3.9g → 0.15g) is a chemically measured record of the 254-year trajectory of USC dilution. The changes in silver content of the Spanish maravedí and the medieval French livre serve the same purpose. This data was used to verify the nonlinearity of USC dilution (a 25-fold reduction in silver content → a 200-fold rise in prices) — the mechanism by which the sequential collapse of the triple sunk cost creates nonlinear amplification was described in 2.1.3.

The USC of fiat currency is not something "invisible" but something that "requires opening multiple windows simultaneously in order to see."

## 2.3 USC (Unit Sunk Cost): Measurement and Dynamic Indicators

If Core Claim 1 ("the value of money is determined by accumulated sunk cost") is a theoretical declaration, this section provides the **measurement instrument** for that declaration. It treats the definition of USC, its measurement, and the methods for tracking its dynamic behavior.

### 2.3.1 Definition and Formula

**USC (Unit Sunk Cost)** is the total cost paid by the current holders of a given currency to acquire it, divided by the total money supply.

> **USC = Total acquisition cost of current holders ÷ Total money supply**

The key to this definition is the condition "current holders." USC reflects not all costs since the currency was first created, but only the costs paid to obtain that currency by those who hold it at this very moment. Accordingly, USC is not a static figure but a dynamic indicator that changes whenever the composition of holders changes.

**There exists only one USC.** Whether the ledger changes or the currency changes, USC does not disappear — it only transfers at an exchange ratio. When gold coins are replaced by banknotes, when banknotes are converted to electronic ledgers, even when hyperinflation collapses the old currency and a new one resets the system, the accumulated sunk cost transfers to the new medium — it is merely revalued at the exchange ratio. This is because USC is not a property of a particular medium but a **property of the monetary phenomenon itself.** This principle of conservation forms the theoretical foundation of the USC crossover model of monetary transitions treated in Layer 6.

### 2.3.2 Bitcoin's Realized Cap — The On-Chain Approximation of USC

Section 2.1.5 described the transparency spectrum of USC measurement, and 2.1.6 treated the proxy system for fiat currency. This subsection treats the specific formula for Bitcoin and its significance.

Bitcoin's **Realized Cap** is the total value obtained by evaluating each UTXO (unspent transaction output) at the price at which it last moved. This is the sum of the prices at which current holders acquired their Bitcoin, and dividing it by the total number of Bitcoin yields the "Realized Price" — that is, the USC of Bitcoin.

> Realized Cap ÷ Total number of Bitcoin = Realized Price ≈ USC

Bitcoin is the first instance in the history of money where USC is measurable in real time, precisely, and transparently without a third party. It provides in a single indicator the precision that combining 6 proxies for fiat currency cannot attain. This transparency enables the predictive power of MVRV, treated in the next subsection.

### 2.3.3 MVRV Ratio — The Quantitative Measure of the Minsky Cycle

The **MVRV Ratio (Market Value to Realized Value)** is the ratio of the market price to USC.

> **MVRV = Market price (Market Cap / total money supply) ÷ USC (Realized Price)**

The significance of this ratio in monetary dynamics is profound. An MVRV of 1.0 means that the market price is equal to the average acquisition cost — that is, on average, holders are at breakeven. The higher the MVRV, the more the market price greatly exceeds the average acquisition cost, meaning that "unrealized gains" have accumulated within the system.

In this theory, MVRV is the quantitative expression of Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis (FIH). What Minsky described only conceptually — the hedge→speculative→Ponzi transition — MVRV captures numerically.

Empirical results from Bitcoin's four cycles (2011, 2013, 2017, 2021):

- MVRV 1.0–1.5: Hedge phase (healthy). Most holders are at breakeven or a small gain.
- MVRV 1.5–2.5: Early speculative phase. Unrealized gains begin accumulating; new entrants increase.
- MVRV 2.5–3.7: Speculative phase (warning). A substantial proportion of holders are in a state of large unrealized gains.
- MVRV >3.7: Ponzi phase (danger). Market price exceeds average acquisition cost by more than 3.7 times.

Instances where MVRV exceeded 3.7 coincided 100% with the peaks of Bitcoin's four cycles; there were zero false positives (cases where MVRV >3.7 but the peak had not occurred). Furthermore, a trend of the MVRV peak declining progressively across cycles was confirmed (7.0→5.5→4.7→3.8). This is evidence of market maturation — more participants recognizing USC and adjusting their behavior accordingly.

**However, the figure 3.7 is an empirical value derived from four Bitcoin cycles and is not a universal threshold.** The trend of the MVRV peak declining across cycles itself demonstrates that this figure is not a fixed constant. In other currencies, different figures may serve as critical thresholds, and even within Bitcoin, this figure may vary in future cycles. What the theory asserts is not a specific figure but a **structural principle** — that if the market price greatly exceeds USC, the system becomes unstable.

It is noteworthy that the existing Bitcoin on-chain analytics community has already been using MVRV as an empirical tool. However, no theoretical explanation for "why MVRV works" was provided. The explanation that this theory provides is as follows: MVRV measures the divergence between market price and USC, and this divergence is the essence of the Minsky cycle. When the market price greatly exceeds USC, holders come to depend on "paper gains" without actual cost investment, and this is isomorphic with Minsky's Ponzi finance.

### 2.3.4 The Multilayered Nature of USC Distribution and Resilience

The USC of a system is not a single average value — it has a distribution. Holders with a variety of USCs coexist, from early low-cost holders to recent high-cost entrants.

This multilayered nature of the distribution determines the resilience of a currency. It is here that we find the reason Bitcoin survives price crashes of 80% or more. Early low-cost holders are still in a state of unrealized gain even when the price falls 80%, so their motivation to sell is weak. They function as an "immune system" that absorbs price declines.

This is directly connected to the critical threshold (treated in detail in Layer 1). Once a currency has secured sufficient multilayered diversity in USC distribution — that is, once a thick distribution of holders who entered at various price levels has formed — that currency can survive large price shocks.

### 2.3.5 Information-Theoretic Interpretation

From the perspective of information theory, USC is isomorphic with **signal fidelity.**

One of the functions of money is to transmit signals of value. The price "this product costs 100 dollars" delivers valid information only when the monetary unit of 100 dollars carries a stable meaning. A currency with high and stable USC has a high Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR). Inflation is the noise mixed into this signal. Hyperinflation is a state in which SNR converges to zero — that is, a state in which money completely loses its function of transmitting value information.

This information-theoretic interpretation explains "why people abruptly switch to foreign currencies or barter in hyperinflation." When the SNR of a signal falls below a certain threshold, it is rational for the receiver (the economic agent) to abandon that channel (the currency) and seek an alternative channel. The use of the U.S. dollar instead of the mark in the Weimar Republic, and the shift from the domestic currency to the rand or the dollar in Zimbabwe, are this mechanism in operation.

The same principle operates even short of hyperinflation. The practice of transacting real estate in Argentina in "metric tons of soybeans" is because the SNR of the peso has fallen below the SNR of soybeans. The noise in the channel called the peso has become too great, and the alternative channel called soybeans provides a more reliable value signal. This is also the operation of "crisis-response niches" treated in Layer 5 — when the SNR of fiat currency falls below the critical threshold, physical goods (soybeans, gold) or alternative currencies (USDT, dollars) substitute for that niche.

## 2.4 Permeating Principle 2: Preservation-Coordination Dynamics

### 2.4.1 Definition

> **"In money there always coexist a force that seeks to preserve the individual's sunk cost (preservation force) and a force that adjusts money at the collective level (coordination force). The equilibrium point of these two forces varies with era, technology, and social structure, and this variation is the driving force of monetary history."**

The preservation force is simple: the force by which one desires to maintain the value of the money earned through one's own labor. It is the motive for saving, the resistance to inflation, and the desire to which the gold standard and Bitcoin's fixed supply appeal.

The coordination force is equally simple: the force that adjusts money at the collective level. It is expressed through diverse motivations — war, famine, pandemic, infrastructure, elite extraction, fiscal stress, and policy failure — and is the motive for taxation, the basis of central bank monetary policy, and the justification for quantitative easing and fiscal deficits.

These two forces are fundamentally incompatible. If individual sunk cost is perfectly preserved (zero inflation, fixed money supply), the collective loses the means to respond to problems at the collective level. If the collective's coordination force is maximized (unlimited money printing), individual sunk cost is obliterated. Every monetary system is located somewhere on this tension, and that location oscillates historically.

### 2.4.2 Root Cause: The Public Goods Problem and the Prisoner's Dilemma

The root cause of the preservation-coordination tension is the public goods problem.

A public good is a good that is non-excludable (one person's use cannot exclude another's) and non-rivalrous (one person's use does not reduce another's). National defense, roads, the legal system, and epidemic prevention are representative examples. These goods are not voluntarily supplied by the market — because everyone seeks to consume without bearing costs (the free-rider problem).

This is a prisoner's dilemma structure. The optimal strategy for each individual is to use public goods without paying taxes, but if all individuals act this way, public goods are not supplied and everyone suffers. Humanity has sequentially invented a series of tools to resolve this dilemma: reciprocity (small communities) → morality/religion (internal compulsion) → custom (social pressure) → law/the state (physical compulsion) → market/brands (economic incentives) → code/protocols (technological compulsion).

Money is the central instrument for procuring public goods and is itself a public good. The monetary system provides society with a "value transmission infrastructure" that is non-excludable and non-rivalrous. This dual status — as a means of procuring public goods while simultaneously being a public good itself — is the source of the preservation-coordination tension.

### 2.4.3 Three Pathways of Public Good Procurement

Historically, the ways in which states procure public goods have evolved along three pathways.

**Pathway 1: Taxation.** The most transparent method. Costs are directly imposed on citizens, and public goods are provided with those costs. Because transparency is high, resistance is also greatest. The principle "no taxation without representation" — which served as the fuse of the American Revolution — is the extreme expression of this resistance.

**Pathway 2: Inflation.** A less transparent method. Additional money is printed, diluting the purchasing power of existing holders, and the difference is used to provide public goods. Unlike taxation, explicit parliamentary approval is not required, and holders find it difficult to immediately recognize the cost burden. Friedman's formulation "inflation is taxation without legislation" precisely captures this mechanism.

**Pathway 3: Government bonds.** The most opaque method. Current funds are raised by pledging future tax revenues as collateral. The cost burden is shifted to future generations, and resistance from the current generation is minimized. U.S. Treasury bonds are the current terminus of this "anti-tax public good procurement" — the largest government bond market in the world is a core piece of infrastructure for the U.S. dollar system, and this is in fact a mechanism that distributes costs across all global dollar holders.

These three pathways align along a single axis: increasing opacity. Taxation is transparent with high resistance; inflation is opaque with low resistance; government bonds are the most opaque with the least resistance. This direction of evolution reveals the inner logic of the coordination force: the coordination force always seeks the path of least resistance, and this means increasing opacity.

### 2.4.4 Preservation-Coordination Oscillation: Extremes Accumulate Pressure in the Opposite Direction

The equilibrium point between preservation force and coordination force is not static. **An extreme in one direction accumulates pressure in the opposite direction, and when this pressure exceeds a threshold, the direction reverses.** This is the mechanism of the preservation-coordination oscillation.

The extreme of preservation force (the rigidity of the gold standard, excessive austerity) worsens economic crises, which creates political momentum for expanding the coordination force — the typical case being the Great Depression of 1929, which triggered the abandonment of the gold standard and the New Deal. The extreme of coordination force (excessive monetary expansion, unlimited quantitative easing) causes inflation and asset bubbles, which creates social demand for a restoration of the preservation force — the typical case being Volcker containing inflation with 20% interest rates in 1979.

The monetary and fiscal history of the United States repeatedly demonstrates this oscillation: gold standard established 1873 (preservation) → gold confiscation and dollar devaluation 1933 (coordination) → Bretton Woods 1944 (structuring of coordination) → end of gold convertibility 1971 (maximization of coordination) → Volcker tightening 1979 (restoration of preservation) → quantitative easing 2008–2020 (maximization of coordination) → tightening 2022 (attempted restoration of preservation). However, the interval of this oscillation is not constant — it ranges from 38 to 71 years with wide variance. This is because the essence of the oscillation is **the accumulation and release of pressure**, not a mechanical cycle.

**Variables that determine the speed of oscillation.** Because the three dimensions of sunk cost (economic, social, cognitive) accumulate at different rates, directional reversals from extremes are also asynchronous. Economic sunk cost responds quickly (prices, interest rates), but social sunk cost (payment networks, trade practices) responds slowly, and cognitive sunk cost ("this currency is safe" as belief) responds most slowly. This difference in speed creates the asymmetry and nonlinearity of the oscillation. External shocks (war, pandemic, forced restructuring) can also abruptly release accumulated pressure and accelerate the transition; conversely, when the triple sunk cost reaches simultaneous extremes, the transition can be extremely delayed (Japan: 35+ years continuing since 1990).

From this perspective, the timing of Bitcoin's birth is not coincidental. 2008 was the moment at which the coordination force had reached an extreme in the United States — quantitative easing, zero interest rates, bailouts of financial institutions. The emergence of Bitcoin in 2009 can be interpreted as the natural emergence (creation) of a technological solution to meet the demand for preservation force tools that had crossed a critical threshold. Bitcoin, before being a "technological innovation," is a "product of the preservation-coordination oscillation."

### 2.4.5 Current Position

According to the analysis of this theory, the current period (the 2020s) corresponds to the late stage of the maximization of coordination force, with the restoration of preservation force having begun. Evidence includes the popularization of cryptocurrency, a trend of declining trust in central banks, the long-term rise in gold prices, and the spread of discourse on "de-dollarization."

However, in accordance with the neutrality principle of this theory, no judgment is made that this is "good" or "bad." If the restoration of preservation force is excessive, it too will give rise to new problems (liquidity crunch, deflationary risk). The theory merely describes the dynamics.

### 2.4.6 Cyclical Expression

This conflict is expressed cyclically through time — preservation dominance → coordination infiltration → coordination dominance → collapse/reset. This four-phase cycle is observed at all scales: a single nation's currency (the recurring crises of the Argentine peso), the reserve currency of an empire (denarius→solidus, pound→dollar), and inter-civilizational transitions (metal→paper→electronic→crypto). The same mechanism operates regardless of scale, exhibiting a self-similar structure. The scale-specific structure (single nation, empire, civilization), technological conditions, and inter-scale interactions of this cycle are treated in detail in Layer 6 (macro-cyclicality).

## 3.1 Layer 0: The Human Condition — The Evolutionary-Biological Foundations of Monetary Phenomena

**Layer 0 is not a layer that adds a degree of freedom. It is the foundation from which all degrees of freedom arise.** Layers 1 through 6 each add a new degree of freedom and express the preservation-coordination conflict upon it, but Layer 0 describes the biological, cognitive, and cultural conditions that make this entire process possible. ### Part A — The Basis of the Preservative Force: Individual-Level Sunk-Cost Tracking

#### 3.1.1 The Primate Legacy: The Evolutionary Origin of Sunk-Cost Tracking

**The question this subsection answers**: Why do humans demand compensation proportional to the costs they have invested? Where does the evolutionary origin of sunk-cost tracking lie?

The deepest roots of monetary phenomena existed before humanity. The capuchin monkey experiment by Frans de Waal and Sarah Brosnan (2003) demonstrated this conclusively.[^1]

The experimental structure was as follows. Two capuchin monkeys sat side by side and performed an identical task (handing a granite token to the experimenter). One received a grape (high-value reward) and the other received a piece of cucumber (low-value reward). The monkey that received the cucumber threw the cucumber at the experimenter and refused to continue the task. Crucially, when the cucumber was presented to the monkey without the other monkey present, this reaction did not occur. **The ability to evaluate one's own reward relative to another's**, and **an emotional rejection response to unfairness** — these two capacities exist before humanity.

Subsequent studies confirmed that this inequity aversion appears most strongly in cooperative species. It is observed in chimpanzees, bonobos, and some macaque species, but not in solitary-living orangutans or squirrel monkeys.[^2] This suggests that inequity aversion is **a mechanism that evolved for the maintenance of cooperation** — the capacity to detect and protest when one's own share in a cooperative arrangement is unjustly small carries adaptive value in preventing exploitation and sustaining long-term cooperative relationships.

It is also worth noting that the monkeys in de Waal's experiment performed a token-exchange task — handing over a small piece of granite and receiving food. This is a primitive but clear **token exchange**, meaning that the cognitive foundations of exchange behavior were already formed before humanity.

**The biological root of the preservative force**: Here lies the evolutionary origin of what the theory calls "attachment to sunk costs" — the psychology of expecting rewards proportional to the costs one has invested. The capuchin monkey's reaction — "I did the same work, so why is my reward different?" — is isomorphic to the human psychological resistance to inflation: "I performed the same labor, so why has the purchasing power of money changed?" The preservative force is not a learned economic rationality but a biological response forged in at least tens of millions of years of primate evolutionary history. However, primate-level inequity aversion is a reaction to immediate, singular events, not a quantitative tracking of accumulated sunk costs. This quantitative tracking mechanism is the subject of the next subsection.

#### 3.1.2 The Cognitive Science of Sunk Costs: Why Humans Cannot Forget the Costs They Have Invested

**The question this subsection answers**: Primate-level inequity aversion is an immediate reaction to a single event. What did humans add on top of this basic mechanism? Specifically, how do humans quantitatively remember costs invested in the past, become attached to them, and allow that attachment to govern present decision-making? What are the neural and cognitive mechanisms that enable the **quantitative and temporal tracking** of sunk costs?

Research answering this question has come primarily from behavioral economics. The interesting point is that these studies were originally conducted with the intent of demonstrating **"human irrationality."** The entire project of Daniel Kahneman, Amos Tversky, and Richard Thaler was to prove "humans are not homo economicus — they deviate systematically from rational models." Yet from the perspective of the present theory, **the very mechanisms they called "irrational biases" are the cognitive foundations upon which money operates.** This reinterpretation is addressed at the end of the subsection. First, let us examine the four mechanisms.

**① Loss Aversion** — Kahneman-Tversky 1979[^3]

Humans feel losses approximately twice as heavily as gains of equivalent size. The pain of losing 100,000 won is twice as great as the joy of gaining 100,000 won. This asymmetry is not learned; it is an evolved response observed in newborns and non-human primates alike. It is the central finding of prospect theory and earned Kahneman the Nobel Prize.

The connection to sunk-cost tracking is direct. Abandoning a cost already invested is perceived as a "loss" and is excessively resisted by loss aversion. The reaction "I've put so much in that I can't walk away now" — what economics calls the "sunk cost fallacy" — originates here. **The asymmetry of loss aversion is why sunk costs "stick."**

**② The Endowment Effect** — Thaler 1980[^4]

People value what they own more highly than an identical object they do not own. In Thaler's classic experiment, coffee mugs were randomly distributed to students; those who received one were asked "how much would you sell it for?" (WTA) and those who did not were asked "how much would you pay for it?" (WTP). WTA was approximately twice WTP. The mere fact of possession elevates perceived value.

One important point: this gap **widens as sunk costs increase.** The more something was actively obtained through one's own effort rather than simply received at random, the stronger the endowment effect. This is exactly consistent with the present theory — **the sunk costs invested in acquisition determine value assessments.**

**③ Mental Accounting** — Thaler 1985[^5]

People do not treat money as a single pool. Instead, they segregate it into psychological accounts such as "food expenses," "leisure," "investment funds," and "emergency savings." A loss in one account is not offset by gains in another — each account operates as an independent unit of value.

This directly connects to the **tripartite sunk-cost** structure of the present theory. The reason that economic, social, and cognitive sunk costs are tracked independently rather than as a single integrated unit of calculation is precisely the operation of mental accounting. Even when economic USC is diluted, social USC ("familiar transaction practices") and cognitive USC ("the belief that this currency is safe") can be maintained separately. The reason that non-linear collapse occurs — because the three accounts decay at different speeds — also derives from here. When the silver content of the denarius decreased twenty-five-fold, prices rose two-hundredfold, because the collapse of economic USC triggered a cascading collapse of social and cognitive USC.

**④ Episodic Memory and Temporal Discounting** — Tulving + Prefrontal Cortex Research[^6]

Endel Tulving's concept of episodic memory shows that human memory is not simply the storage of facts but rather **the replay of specific events with temporal location**. "At time T, in place P, I invested this cost" — this concrete, temporally anchored memory is what makes sunk-cost tracking possible. Without episodic memory, the structure of "when and how much was invested" does not hold, and therefore the temporal accumulation of USC does not hold either.

The temporal discounting capacity of the prefrontal cortex extends this episodic memory in the forward direction. The capacity to pay a present cost for a future reward — that is, the triple temporal structure of "invest now and recover later" — is impossible without it. The fact that the human prefrontal cortex is the most hypertrophied among primates is the biological basis of this capacity.

**The decisive reframing**: Behavioral economics calls the above mechanisms **"irrational biases."** The expression "sunk cost fallacy" encapsulates this perspective — attachment to costs already paid is an error in rational decision-making. A homo economicus should treat sunk costs as past and calculate only present and future marginal utility.

**The present theory inverts this interpretation.** What behavioral economics calls "bias" is in fact **an evolved cognitive mechanism necessary for money to operate**. Loss aversion, the endowment effect, mental accounting, sunk-cost attachment — without these, USC does not accumulate, and therefore money cannot exist either. In a society composed solely of humans who are not attached to sunk costs (perfect homo economicus), money cannot exist. Because no one evaluates value by reference to "the costs I have already invested," no token can accumulate value.

The psychological rigidity that Kahneman called "error" is the biological foundation of the social institution called money. The findings of behavioral economics are not discarded in the present theory but **reinterpreted** — at the individual level they are "deviations from rationality," but at the collective level they are "indispensable mechanisms that make money possible."

**Refinement of the preservative force**: If 3.1.1's primate legacy showed the evolutionary prototype of the preservative force, 3.1.2 shows what humans added on top of that prototype — quantitative tracking (loss aversion), attachment to ownership (the endowment effect), categorical segregation (mental accounting), temporal extension (episodic memory + temporal discounting). The combination of these four mechanisms produces the distinctly human capacity for **quantitative and temporal sunk-cost tracking**. The preservative force is the psychological expression of this capacity. (The market-level effects of these foundational findings — self-referential cycles, Minsky's FIH, MVRV — are addressed in Layer 4. Layer 0 concerns the evolutionary and cognitive origins of the mechanisms; Layer 4 concerns the self-reinforcing loops those mechanisms generate at the market level.)

### Part B — The Basis of the Coordinative Force: Group-Level Cooperation and Public Goods

---

#### 3.1.3 Ultrasociality and Public Goods: The Evolutionary Origin of Large-Scale Cooperation

**The question this subsection answers**: Why are humans capable of providing public goods and engaging in coordination behavior within large-scale groups of non-kin? What is the evolutionary basis for the dilution of individual accumulation through taxation, conscription, and inflation?

If the preservative force is the force by which individuals seek to protect their own sunk costs, the coordinative force is the force that dilutes individual accumulation at the collective level. These two forces are symmetrical. The basis of the preservative force is relatively clear — it derives directly from individual evolved cognitive mechanisms. The basis of the coordinative force, however, is more subtle. **Why do individuals surrender their accumulated wealth contrary to their own interests?** Simple coercion alone is insufficient as an explanation — behavior of contributing to public goods is consistently observed even in the absence of coercion.

What answers this puzzle is the study of **human ultrasociality**. Humans are the only species in the animal kingdom that cooperates with millions of unrelated strangers. Chimpanzees also cooperate, but their cooperation is limited to kin and direct reciprocity. Large-scale cooperation in other species (ants, bees) is explained by kin selection — because members are genetically nearly identical. Humans belong to neither category. **The capacity to cooperate with millions of genetically unrelated individuals is unique to Homo sapiens.**

**① Ultrasociality and Shared Intentionality** — Michael Tomasello[^7]

Tomasello's comparative cognitive research revealed that human infants exhibit, from the first year of life, an ability that decisively differs from that of chimpanzees: **shared intentionality** — the awareness that "we are doing this together." Chimpanzees can perceive the intentions of others but cannot form a first-person plural perspective called "we." Humans form this perspective from early childhood, and it becomes the cognitive basis for large-scale cooperation and the pursuit of shared goals. The coordinative force is the evolutionary extension of this "we" perspective — from family to tribe, from tribe to city-state, from city-state to empire.

**② Unconditional Contribution in Public-Goods Games** — Fehr-Gächter 2002[^8]

The public-goods game experiments of Ernst Fehr and Simon Gächter quantitatively confirmed this. The standard economic model predicts "rational free-riding" in public-goods games — contribute nothing oneself, while benefiting only from others' contributions. Yet in actual experiments, participants contributed an average of 40–60% of their resources to the public good. They contributed even in the first round, when no punishment option was available. This is evidence that a **congenital disposition toward cooperation** is at work, not rational calculation.

More remarkably: when a punishment option is introduced, contribution levels are maintained at higher levels. Some participants incur personal costs to punish free riders, and this punishment sustains the contributions of other participants. This is the subject of the next subsection (3.1.4) — **altruistic punishment**.

**③ Strong Reciprocity and Group Selection** — Bowles-Gintis[^9]

Samuel Bowles and Herbert Gintis named this tendency **strong reciprocity**: conditional cooperation combined with punishment. Humans cooperate when others cooperate, and punish defectors at personal cost. This is a third disposition incompatible with both the standard economic model of rational self-interest and the kin-centered altruism model.

Bowles and Gintis propose **cultural group selection** as the evolutionary explanation for this disposition. Groups with strong reciprocity had superior internal cooperation and prevailed in intergroup competition; as a result, this disposition was selected at the group level and became fixed. Individual-level selection alone cannot evolve the behavior of incurring personal costs to punish — group-level selection is required. This is currently the principal explanation for the evolutionary puzzle of large-scale human cooperation.

**④ The Scale of Ultrasociality** — Peter Turchin, Joseph Henrich[^10]

Peter Turchin and Joseph Henrich traced how human cooperation scaled up over tens of thousands of years. The expansion from hunter-gatherer bands (~150 people) to tribal confederacies (~thousands), to chiefdoms (~tens of thousands), to early states (~hundreds of thousands), to empires (~tens of millions), and to modern states (~hundreds of millions) required new coordination mechanisms at each stage (religion, writing, money, bureaucracy, law). Money is one of the key tools of this expansion. And **money itself is a product of the coordinative force** — only species capable of large-scale coordination can invent and sustain money.

**The biological roots of the coordinative force**: What the above studies collectively demonstrate is that humans possess an **evolved disposition toward group-oriented behavior** that transcends individual selfish calculation. This disposition is not learned but innate, and it was reinforced over tens of thousands of years by cultural group selection. This is the biological root of the coordinative force. The reason people resist taxation yet ultimately pay, complain about conscription yet ultimately serve, rage against inflation yet do not bring the system to collapse — behind all of these "ultimately complying" responses lies an instinct for group cooperation evolved over tens of thousands of years.

**The irresolvability of the preservation-coordination conflict**: Here the core claim of the theory becomes clear. The preservative force is rooted in individual evolved cognitive mechanisms (3.1.1–3.1.2), and the coordinative force is rooted in the group's evolved cooperative mechanisms (3.1.3). **Both forces are biological instincts forged through tens of thousands of years of evolution.** Therefore no institutional design can eliminate either one. A pure free market that retains only the preservative force is impossible (humans have an instinct to contribute to public goods), and pure central planning that retains only the coordinative force is equally impossible (humans have an instinct to attach to sunk costs). Both extremes collide with human nature. This is the fundamental reason the preservation-coordination conflict is "irresolvable."

#### 3.1.4 Norm Enforcement: Altruistic Punishment Enforces the Coordinative Force

**The question this subsection answers**: Even with a disposition toward ultrasociality and public-goods contribution, this alone is insufficient to keep cooperation stable in large-scale groups. When free riders appear, cooperation collapses. What suppresses free-riding and stabilizes the operation of the coordinative force?

The Fehr-Gächter experiment of 3.1.3 already offered a partial answer. When a **punishment option** is added to the public-goods game, contribution levels are maintained or increase, because some participants incur personal costs to punish free riders. Fehr named this **altruistic punishment**.[^11]

The crux is this: punishers consume their own resources. Punishment imposes costs on free riders, but it also imposes costs on the punisher themselves. Standard rational-choice models cannot explain this behavior — it is individually advantageous not to punish. Yet experimentally, a substantial proportion of participants consistently display punishment behavior. And **the very possibility of punishment suppresses free-riding**, thereby sustaining cooperation across the group.

**Altruistic punishment is the enforcement mechanism of the coordinative force.** The coordinative force requires the dilution of individual accumulation. For this dilution to operate, those who resist it (free riders) must be punished. Without this punishment, rational individuals would all attempt to free-ride, and the coordinative force would immediately collapse. The evolution of altruistic punishment is the key mechanism guaranteeing the evolutionary stability of the coordinative force.

**Concrete operation in monetary systems**: The monetary system is itself a public good. It provides the social infrastructure of "value transmission" in a non-excludable, non-rivalrous manner. The maintenance of this public good requires altruistic punishment — reporting counterfeiters, refusing fraudulent transactions, severing trade with those who violate trust, socially condemning tax evaders. All of these coercive behaviors impose costs on the enforcer yet are carried out nonetheless.

**In-group bias and monetary nationalism**: The finding that the prosocial effects of oxytocin operate strongly within the in-group but weakly toward out-groups (De Dreu et al., 2010)[^12] carries interesting implications. Altruistic punishment and public-goods contributions operate primarily **within the same group**. This explains why currencies are differentiated at the **level of the nation-state**, why domestic payments are more stable than international settlements, and why monetary nationalism persists tenaciously. The operational range of the coordinative force coincides with the range of the "we" sense.

**The maintenance mechanism of social USC**: Of the tripartite sunk costs in the present theory, **social sunk costs** — payment networks, transaction practices, credit reputation — are social capital that is collectively accumulated and maintained by the group. This capital is maintained only when free-riding is effectively suppressed. In a society where altruistic punishment does not operate, social USC is rapidly eroded by the self-interested defection of individuals. The strong correlation between national-level trust and economic growth (Zak & Knack, 2001; Fehr, 2009)[^13] is the macro-level evidence of this connection.[^oh-trust-civilization]

[^oh-trust-civilization]: Taemin Oh argues that civilization operates through the "invisible order" of trust embodied in money and law, and addresses the structural contradictions of dollar hegemony and the Triffin Dilemma in the context of trust collapse. Oh Taemin et al., *A Future Without Bitcoin Is Impossible* (Geoin's Garden, 2025).

The stability of a monetary system is not determined solely by the magnitude of its economic sunk costs. Without the enforcement mechanism that maintains social sunk costs — that is, without the operation of altruistic punishment — even the largest economic sunk cost cannot prevent the system's collapse. The collapse of money in failed states is often due to the disintegration of this mechanism.

### Part C — The Emergence of Money: Where the Two Forces Intersect

#### 3.1.5 Cognitive Limits: The Social Brain and Dunbar's Number

**The question this subsection answers**: Parts A and B have separately established the biological bases of the preservative and coordinative forces. What, then, are the specific conditions under which these two forces intersect and **money** emerges? Why is money unnecessary in small-scale groups and becomes necessary at a certain point?

The answer lies in **cognitive limits**. In small-scale groups, the individual tracking of Part A and the group cooperation of Part B are integrated through direct relationships and reputation — one can "know" who is trustworthy, who will repay debts, and who free-rides. But once a group exceeds a certain size, this direct tracking becomes cognitively impossible.

Robin Dunbar's Social Brain Hypothesis defines this critical threshold.[^14] There is a strong positive correlation between the size of the neocortex in primates and the size of their stable social groups. The upper limit on stable social relationships predicted from human brain size is approximately 150. This number — Dunbar's Number — has been confirmed not by abstract calculation but by a broad range of empirical evidence: the size of hunter-gatherer clans, the size of Roman legionary centuries, the size of Neolithic villages, the size of modern individuals' Christmas card mailing lists, and the median number of Facebook friends all fall within this range.[^15]

This relationship forms a fractal structure: 5 (core support group) — 15 (close friends) — 50 (good friends) — 150 (meaningful relationships) — 500 (acquaintances) — 1,500 (people whose faces are known). The quality of relationships and the time invested differ at each tier, and this structure is observed identically in online social networks.[^16]

The implications of this limit for monetary theory are profound. **Below 150**, the individual tracking mechanisms of Part A and the group cooperation mechanisms of Part B **integrate through direct relationships and reputation**. Each person can remember what they invested in whom, observe who contributes to the public good, and directly punish free riders. The debt relationships described by Graeber (2011) — "I gave you meat, so pay me back later" — operate in this domain. Money is unnecessary.

**Once 150 is exceeded, this integration breaks down.** Situations arise in which one must exchange with someone one does not personally know, and it becomes impossible to track past histories of cooperation. The sunk-cost tracking of Part A continues to operate but **must be externalized**, and the group cooperation of Part B continues to operate but **must be mediated indirectly**. It is at precisely this point that money emerges.

**Money = an externalization tool at the intersection of the two forces**: Money externalizes the sunk costs of Part A from memory into tokens, and transforms the group cooperation of Part B from direct observation into an indirect signal (token possession). Rather than needing to remember "was this person cooperative in the past?", one need only verify "does this person hold the token?" The token is the physical condensation of past cooperative acts and the sunk costs invested therein.

#### 3.1.6 Emergence at the Limit: The Moment a Token Becomes Money

**The question this subsection answers**: Beyond Dunbar's Number, money becomes theoretically necessary. But is this emergence actually observed in the laboratory? Does a token spontaneously acquire monetary function without a designer?

Gabriele Camera's experiment empirically demonstrated this under controlled conditions.[^17]

Experimental design: Participants were divided into small-scale (2-person) or large-scale (N>2) groups and made to repeat a helping game. Providing help incurred a cost, but receiving help produced a benefit. Crucially, **the person providing help and the person receiving it changed every round** — direct reciprocity ("you helped me, so I'll help you") was impossible.

**Result 1**: In small-scale groups, reputation-based cooperation worked. "That person helped me last time, so I'll help them." But as the group grew larger, cooperation collapsed even though monitoring and rewards were held constant. The cognitive limit corresponding to Dunbar's Number was reproduced in the laboratory as well.

**Result 2**: Intrinsically worthless tokens were then introduced. Something remarkable happened — **the tokens spontaneously became money.** Participants began demanding tokens in exchange for help and providing help to those who held tokens. And **cooperation levels remained stable even as the group grew larger**.

**Result 3**: Analysis using evolutionary models proves that token-based exchange (monetary trade) is an **Evolutionarily Stable Strategy (ESS)**. When the population is heterogeneous — when cooperators and defectors are mixed — cooperation collapses without tokens, but with tokens cooperation is maintained stably.

This experiment empirically demonstrates the first core claim of the theory in its purest form. The tokens in the laboratory have zero intrinsic value — plastic pieces that cannot be eaten or used for anything. There is no state declaration, no metallic scarcity. **The properties of the medium are entirely absent.** Yet the moment participants invest labor to obtain those tokens (the moment they provide help), sunk costs accumulate in the token and the token becomes money. **The claim that value is created not by the properties of the medium but by accumulated sunk costs has been reproduced under controlled experimental conditions.**

**Three theoretical implications**: First, **money is not "invented" but "emergent"** — it arises naturally when conditions are met, without anyone designing it. Second, **money becomes behaviorally essential when Dunbar's Number is exceeded** — it is not a convenient tool but a necessary condition for large-scale cooperation. Third, **the token is the externalized junction of Part A (sunk costs) and Part B (group cooperation)** — the medium through which individual input (sunk costs) is converted into a collective signal (token possession).

### Part D — The Persistence of Money and Historical Verification

---

#### 3.1.7 Cultural Amplification: The Intergenerational Transmission of Monetary Custom

**The question this subsection answers**: Once money has emerged, how does it propagate across generations and accumulate? What is the mechanism by which monetary customs are sustained over timescales longer than an individual's biological lifespan?

In Camera's experiment, tokens spontaneously became money, but the participants were already Homo sapiens who know what money is. For this emergence to be sustained across generations **in a state where the concept of money does not yet exist**, a mechanism is needed for propagating the custom that "this is a medium of exchange." Joseph Henrich's theory of gene-culture co-evolution explains this.[^18]

According to Henrich, humans are a species in which two distinctive capacities have co-evolved. First, **cumulative cultural learning** — the capacity for knowledge to accumulate and be refined across generations. Other species also engage in social learning, but cumulative refinement is unique to humans. Second, **norm psychology** — the psychological tendency to internalize a group's norms. These two capacities are not genetically fixed but are the products of co-evolution in which cultural environment and genetic evolution each drive the other.

The Ultimatum Game experiments Henrich conducted across fifteen small-scale societies empirically demonstrate this relationship.[^19] Societies with higher market integration made more equitable offers. This suggests that monetary exchange promoted gene-culture co-evolution in a direction that reinforced human norms of fairness. On top of de Waal's primate-level sense of fairness, the cultural practice of monetary exchange created stronger fairness norms.

**The origin of cognitive sunk cost**: Here lies the origin of the **cognitive sunk costs** in the theory's tripartite structure. The cross-generational belief that "the dollar is safe" or "gold is valuable" is not something accumulated over a single lifetime by any individual; it has been transmitted through cumulative cultural learning. This cognitive accumulation is the least visible yet the most powerful — it is the inertia of this cultural accumulation that explains why Russians continue to use the ruble "with distaste but out of habit" even after hyperinflation, and why the dollar continues to be treated as a "safe reserve asset" even as its real purchasing power declines. The collapse of cognitive USC occurs only in the final stage of hyperinflation, and when it occurs, it is the most abrupt.

#### 3.1.8 Historical Context: Debt Comes First; Money Is an Extension of Debt

**The question this subsection answers**: Most of the biological and experimental evidence presented thus far comes from laboratory conditions. How did money actually emerge in history? Is Menger's barter-origin thesis confirmed in reality?

David Graeber's anthropological research provides the historical context of actual reality for all of the biological and experimental findings above.[^20]

The strata above — primate fairness, the cognitive science of sunk costs, ultrasociality and public goods, altruistic punishment, Dunbar's Number, the spontaneous emergence of tokens, cultural norms — were derived from the laboratory and from theory. But how did money actually emerge in human societies? What Graeber showed through thousands of archaeological and anthropological pieces of evidence is this: **Menger's assumption that barter is the universal state preceding money has never been confirmed at any anthropological field site.** The actual form of exchange before money was the debt relationship — "I gave you something, so pay me back later."

This converges precisely with Camera's experiment. In Camera's experiment as well, what operates first in small-scale groups is debt-based reciprocity, and the token (money) appears when this reciprocity reaches its limits due to group expansion. Graeber's historical observation and Camera's experimental result point to the same conclusion: **money is not a substitute for debt but an extension of it.**

In addition, the relationship between money and violence that Graeber emphasized — that the emergence of minted coins is synchronized with large-scale military operations — connects to the discussion of ultrasociality in Part B. In early large-scale states, money emerged as a tool for procuring public goods (military public goods in particular). The pattern by which the coordinative force takes over monetary institutions is addressed in detail at Layer 2 ("private-organic emergence → state appropriation").

**Reconfirmation of the co-evolutionary relationship**: One important caveat — all of the strata of Layer 0 **must not be read as linear causation**. This is not a unidirectional causal chain of "primate fairness → cognitive science → ultrasociality → Dunbar's Number → monetary emergence." As Henrich emphasizes, this is a **co-evolutionary relationship** in which culture changes genes and genes change culture. Monetary exchange may have reinforced cognitive systems, and ultrasociality may have made money possible. Graeber's "debt comes first" converges precisely with the USC concept of the present theory — debt relationships are sunk costs tracked on a personal basis, and money is what that tracking is externalized into as impersonal tokens at the moment it exceeds Dunbar's Number.

#### 3.1.9 Synthesis: The Biological Foundations of the Core Claims

The purpose of developing Layer 0 through Parts A to D was to show that both of the theory's core claims stand on evolved biological foundations.

**The foundation of Core Claim 1 (sunk cost determines monetary value) — Part A:**

Humans evolved to track sunk costs. This tracking began with primate-level inequity aversion (3.1.1) and was refined through distinctly human cognitive mechanisms — loss aversion, the endowment effect, mental accounting, episodic memory (3.1.2) — into quantitative and temporal tracking. What behavioral economics calls "irrational bias" is, from the perspective of the present theory, an evolved necessary condition for money to operate. That sunk costs form the basis of monetary value is not a cultural accident but an evolved biological necessity: **humans cannot help but track sunk costs.**

The origin of the tripartite sunk costs (economic, social, cognitive) is also clarified here. Economic sunk costs (labor and resource investment) are rooted in primate-level exchange behavior and loss aversion; social sunk costs (relationships and reputation) are rooted in altruistic punishment and public-goods mechanisms (3.1.4); cognitive sunk costs (beliefs and culture) are rooted in cumulative cultural learning (3.1.7). Mental accounting (3.1.2) provides the cognitive rationale for why these three accounts are tracked independently.

**The foundation of Core Claim 2 (the preservation-coordination conflict drives the dynamics) — Part B:**

The preservative force is rooted in individual cognitive mechanisms (Part A). The coordinative force is rooted in the group's ultrasociality mechanisms (Part B). **Both forces are biological instincts forged through tens of thousands of years of evolution.** Ultrasociality, shared intentionality, strong reciprocity, altruistic punishment, cultural group selection — all of these mechanisms made humans "the only species capable of large-scale non-kin cooperation." Here lies the evolutionary basis for the dilution of individual accumulation through taxation, conscription, and inflation.

**Why the preservation-coordination conflict is irresolvable** now becomes clear. Because both forces are evolved instincts, no institutional design can eliminate either one. A pure free market (eliminating the coordinative force) and pure central planning (eliminating the preservative force) both collide with human nature. The conflict can be managed but cannot be resolved. This is not a failure of institutional design but a structural feature of human nature.

**The conditions for the emergence of money — Part C:**

Even with the two instincts of the preservative and coordinative forces, money is unnecessary in small-scale groups, because the two mechanisms are integrated through direct relationships and reputation. Money becomes **behaviorally essential** when the cognitive limit of Dunbar's Number (~150) is exceeded. Camera's experiment reproduced this emergence under controlled conditions and showed that even tokens with zero intrinsic value become money once sunk costs accumulate in them. This is the purest empirical demonstration of Core Claim 1.

**Persistence and historical verification — Part D:**

Once it has emerged, money propagates across generations through cumulative cultural learning (3.1.7) and is maintained stably in combination with altruistic punishment (3.1.4). Graeber's historical evidence (3.1.8) shows that this emergence path — personal debt (memory-based USC) → impersonal tokens (externalized USC) — is confirmed in actual history as well. Money did not come from barter; it came from debt. The theoretical predictions of the present theory and the anthropological reality converge.

**Layer 0 is not a degree of freedom.** As repeatedly emphasized above, Layer 0 is the **foundation** from which the degrees of freedom of Layers 1 through 6 can arise. On this foundation, each layer adds a new degree of freedom, and the same two principles (sunk cost and preservation-coordination) express themselves in different forms at each layer. The subsequent subsections examine these degrees of freedom one by one.

## 3.2 Layer 1: Adoption

**Degree of freedom:** The degree of freedom that Layer 1 adds is **medium selection**. Without this degree of freedom, one cannot describe "why stone rather than gold, why Bitcoin rather than Litecoin." Layer 0 established the biological basis for the occurrence of monetary phenomena — humans evolved to track sunk costs (Part A) and evolved to enable group coordination (Part B). Layer 1 addresses **which medium begins to accumulate USC** on this foundation.

What Core Claim 1 ("the value of money is determined not by the properties of the medium but by accumulated sunk costs") means in the context of adoption is: **it is not the physical properties of the medium that "determine" adoption, but rather that a medium capable of accumulating USC is adopted.** The question then becomes: in which medium does USC begin to accumulate? Why gold rather than brass? Why cowrie rather than another shell? Why Bitcoin rather than Litecoin?

### What Gets Selected

Carl Menger argued that "saleability" (Absatzfähigkeit) is the determining variable of money — the most marketable commodity becomes money. The present theory revises this. Saleability is the **result** of adoption, not the **cause**. Gold is saleable because gold is money, not the other way around. Cause and effect are inverted.

What, then, are the actual determinants of adoption? Within the USC framework, three are identified.

**① Observability of Acquisition Cost**

If USC determines monetary value, participants must be **able to perceive** that "costs have been invested in this medium." If USC is invisible, others cannot assess the value of the medium.

- **Gold**: Everyone knows that mining requires enormous labor. The weight and luster of gold are the physical evidence of mining effort. Technologies for verifying purity (touchstone, specific gravity measurement) have existed for thousands of years.
- **Rai of Yap**: The danger and labor of voyaging hundreds of kilometers to Palau to quarry limestone and transport it by raft back to Yap are **observable by the entire village**. Everyone remembers who undertook what journey and who was injured in the process. The value of rai is determined not only by size but by **the story of the acquisition process**.
- **Bitcoin**: Proof of Work is a mechanism that **mathematically certifies** acquisition costs. The fact that miners invested electricity and hardware is permanently recorded on the blockchain. For the first time in history, acquisition costs exist in a form verifiable without a third party.
- **Prison cigarettes**: In prisons, the acquisition costs of cigarettes (commissary purchase price, social costs invested in exchange) are naturally observable within the confines of the small community.

O'Keefe's Yap stone case is the decisive evidence for this principle. O'Keefe used Western ships to bring large quantities of rai with ease. Physically, these were limestone identical to existing rai. Yet the people of Yap refused to treat them as money — **because the acquisition costs were observably low**. A stone brought easily without dangerous voyaging contains no sunk costs, and without sunk costs there is no value. This is direct empirical evidence for Core Claim 1.

**② Focal Point: Social Convergence**

If there are multiple media capable of accumulating USC, society must **converge on one of them**. Thomas Schelling called such a point of convergence a focal point — "the point I expect others to select, and which others expect me to expect."

Focal points are not entirely random. Certain properties facilitate convergence:
- **Cultural salience**: In the case of cowrie shells, they had religious and aesthetic value in West Africa. This cultural significance became the trigger for convergence around "treating this as valuable."
- **Technological primacy**: Bitcoin was the first permissionless digital scarcity. The very fact of being "first" formed the focal point. Subsequent forks and alternative coins, even if technically similar or superior, have been unable to overcome this "first-mover" focal point.

**"The first spark is close to accident, but what spreads is determined by conditions."** There is considerable contingency in the initial formation of a focal point, but once the focal point is formed, USC accumulation locks in the selection. The fact that Bitcoin Cash (BCH) copied Bitcoin's (BTC) code exactly yet failed to copy even 1% of its value is the clearest evidence of this dynamic. A fork replicates code and token numbers but cannot replicate sunk costs — while the combined market capitalization of the two chains momentarily appeared to increase, since the actual invested costs remained unchanged, they converged rapidly to one side. **Technology can be copied, but sunk costs cannot.**

**③ Contextual Availability**

No matter how theoretically good a medium is, if it is **not accessible** in the given environment, it cannot be selected.

- In prisons, where external currency cannot be brought in, selection occurs among goods purchasable at the commissary (instant noodles, cigarettes). Instant noodles have an expiration date and are not "non-perishable" in an absolute sense, but within the time horizon of the prison economy (weeks to months), they persist sufficiently for sunk cost accumulation to occur. **"Non-perishability" is not an absolute physical property but is relative to the time horizon of the economy in question.**
- In West Africa, cowrie shells were imported via Indian Ocean trade routes. The geographical distance between the source of cowries (the Maldives) and their place of use (West Africa) created a natural supply constraint.
- In the digital age, cryptocurrencies become available as options wherever internet access exists. Where there is no internet, only physical media are possible.

Contextual availability is also a framework for reinterpreting the conditions Menger enumerated (divisibility, portability, durability, etc.). These conditions are not "necessary requirements" for money but rather **environmental variables that determine how smoothly sunk cost accumulation proceeds in a given context**. The Yap rai is virtually impossible to transport yet functions as money — because in the small-scale community of Yap, a protocol of oral title transfer compensates for the absence of portability. Divisibility, portability, and uniformity affect the **speed and convenience** of sunk cost accumulation but do not determine whether something can function as money. **Context (sunk costs) matters more than conditions.**

### Historical Cases — The Operation of the Three Factors

| Medium | Observability of Acquisition Cost | Focal Point | Contextual Availability |
|--------|----------------------------------|------------|------------------------|
| **Gold** | Mining labor physically verifiable by weight and luster; purity verifiable by touchstone | The only metal that does not corrode; its luster forms visual salience | Distributed globally in ore deposits; transferable between civilizations |
| **Cowrie** | Labor of collection and transport; trade distance from the Indian Ocean to West Africa | Religious and aesthetic cultural value; natural uniformity | Inflow via Indian Ocean trade routes |
| **Rai of Yap** | Danger and labor of the Palau voyage remembered by the whole village | Visual uniqueness of massive limestone + shared narrative of acquisition | Existence of the Palau-Yap sea route |
| **Prison instant noodles** | Commissary purchase price observable within the community | The most in-demand items (Marlboros, Shin Ramyun) form the focal point | Limited to items purchasable at the prison commissary |
| **Bitcoin** | PoW mathematically certifies electricity and hardware investment | The first permissionless digital scarcity | Worldwide, wherever internet access is available |

In every case, the physical properties of the medium are **part** of the conditions, not the determining variable. The determining variable is **the combination of structural conditions enabling sunk cost to accumulate**. O'Keefe's rai (physically identical, no sunk cost → rejected) and prison instant noodles (not non-perishable, yet sunk cost can accumulate → adopted) illustrate this most starkly.

Bitcoin is also **the first case in history in which the adoption process can be observed in real time**. The adoption process of traditional money (gold, cowrie) was completed in prehistory and cannot be observed, but Bitcoin's entire adoption process since its birth in 2009 — initial worthlessness, focal-point formation, sunk cost accumulation, survival of four major collapses (94%, 85%, 84%, 77%) — is recorded on the blockchain. By contrast, Terra-Luna (market capitalization of $40 billion) collapsed within 72 hours after scaling up without actual invested sunk costs (algorithmic issuance). As of 2025, 53% of all cryptocurrency projects have disappeared, the great majority being tokens created without actual cost investment — **the presence or absence of sunk cost is the dividing line between survival and extinction**.

### Transition to the Next Layer

Once a particular medium is selected at Layer 1 and USC begins to accumulate, a new dimension opens around that medium: **ledger authority** (who issues, who verifies, who can change the rules, who can access). The USC accumulated at Layer 1 becomes the object of the preservation-coordination conflict beginning at Layer 2 — individuals seek to protect their accumulated sunk costs (the preservative force) while the force that adjusts money at the collective level (the coordinative force) seeks to dilute them. The full unfolding of this conflict is the story of Layer 2 and beyond.

## 3.3 Layer 2: Ledger Authority — The Technical Properties of the Medium Constrain the Channels Through Which the Preservation-Coordination Conflict Manifests

### Core Perspective

**The definition of "ledger" must first be made precise.** Here, "ledger" is not limited to literal written records. It encompasses the full range of tracking systems for ownership and transactions — the oral agreements of Yap Island, the clay tablets of Sumer, the paper ledgers of banks, the electronic databases of central banks, and the blockchain of Bitcoin. On Yap Island, the fact that every member of the village remembers "that Rai stone belongs to so-and-so" is itself a ledger.[^oh-ledger]

[^oh-ledger]: Taemin Oh proposed viewing money as a ledger system and traces the humanistic genealogy of money through history. This "money as ledger" perspective is the starting point for this theory's Layer 2 (Ledger Authority). Oh Taemin, *Bitcoin: The Genealogy of Wisdom* (Geoin's Garden, 2020).

Because the preservation-coordination conflict originates in human nature (Layer 0), it can never be fully resolved. This conflict, established in Layers 0 through 1, is invariant. **What changes is the technical and physical properties of the medium, and these constrain the form in which the conflict manifests.** Medium technology makes certain authority structures impossible and incentivizes others, yet within those constraints, considerable diversity remains. On the same metallic money, both the Lydian model of decentralized minting and the Roman model of state monopoly were possible. Technology does not "determine" the path — it "constrains" it.

Solving the same optimization problem (preservation vs. coordination) under different constraints (gold, paper, electronic, cryptographic) yields different solutions:

| Medium | Location of Preservation Force | Location of Coordination Force | Outcome |
|--------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------|---------|
| **Gold** (heavy, high verification cost, high mining cost) | Naturally strong — issuance cost protects USC | Weak — physically difficult to produce in quantity | Delegation of verification is the sole entry point for coordination force |
| **Paper** (light, easy to counterfeit, low issuance cost) | Weakened — relies on issuance monopoly for counterfeit prevention | Strengthened — issuance is physically easy | Issuance monopoly becomes inevitable. Fractional reserves become possible. |
| **Electronic ledger** (zero issuance cost, infinite copying) | Extremely weak — restraint depends entirely on willpower | Extremely strong — physical constraints on issuance disappear | Restraint based on willpower has historically always failed |
| **Cryptography** (mathematical scarcity, permissionless verification) | Enforced by code — independent of human will | Blocked at the source — issuance rules cannot be altered | The technological counter-offensive of preservation force. Coordination force is redirected through access channels |

Layer 2 is the intersection where human nature meets the material properties of the medium. If Layer 1 addressed "what becomes money," Layer 2 addresses "how authority over the same medium is allocated." Without this degree of freedom, one cannot explain "why the same metal gave rise to completely different monetary dynamics across different eras."

### The Four Components of Ledger Authority

Every monetary ledger has four operational authorities:

| Authority | Question |
|-----------|----------|
| Issuance | Who creates new units |
| Validation | Who recognizes transactions as valid |
| Amendment | Who can change the rules |
| Access | Who can access the ledger |

The four authorities are interdependent with no hierarchy among them. However, access authority functions as the most immediate lever over the other three — if access is blocked, issuance, validation, and amendment are all rendered ineffective. The fact that the 2022 SWIFT cut-off against Russia (access authority) produced more immediate effects than Russia's ruble issuance rules (issuance authority) or the Russian central bank's policy decisions (amendment authority) is empirical evidence of this dynamic. At the same time, access authority is the point at which coordination force is most covertly exercised. Issuance, validation, and amendment change the size of USC, but access restrictions create path-specific friction, causing the observed price of the same currency to diverge across channels.

Throughout history, in every monetary system, authority and trust have been inseparable. Granting issuance authority to someone meant trusting that person. The fundamental change brought by code-based implementation (Bitcoin) is the separation of authority from trust — Bitcoin's miners hold issuance authority, yet we do not trust the miners. If a miner violates the rules, the network automatically rejects the transaction.

### Access Friction (μ)

Access friction is an original concept of this theory. It may appear similar to transaction cost but is crucially different. Transaction cost is the cost attached to the transaction itself (fees, spreads, information costs), whereas μ is **the difference in access costs across pathways to the same currency**. The fact that the same dollar behaves differently when withdrawn from a U.S. bank (μ ≈ 0) versus a Lebanese bank (μ ≈ 0.8) is not a difference in transaction costs but a structural difference in access pathways.

μ is not a variable that measures the causes of friction (why friction is high) but rather **its effects (what happens when friction is high)** — it is a categorical tool for ordinal comparison rather than precise numerical measurement:

- μ ≈ 0: No friction (same-bank transfer, BTC on-chain)
- μ low: International remittance, currency exchange
- μ medium: Formal channels under capital controls
- μ high: Informal channels (washing machine purchases, underground exchange, hawala, iPhone relay)
- μ ≈ 1: Effectively impossible (North Korean won used abroad)

The point at which μ's explanatory power exceeds that of simple transaction costs: **it systematically explains multiple prices for the same currency**. In Lebanon in 2019, bank-deposit dollars (lollars) traded at an 80% discount to physical dollars. The explanation of "loss of trust" is a redescription of the phenomenon, not an explanation. μ provides a structural explanation: Lebanese dollars had at least four access pathways (bank deposits μ ≈ 0.8, checks μ ≈ 0.5, market cash μ ≈ 0.1, overseas transfer μ ≈ 0.05), and because each pathway had a different μ, the same "dollar" traded at four different prices. The CNY/CNH spread (difference in onshore/offshore friction), the GBTC discount (μ of the redemption pathway), and the lollar (μ of dollars trapped in banks) are all explained within this framework.

When friction is high, two reactions occur simultaneously. **Segmentation reaction** (μ ≈ 1): effectively separate markets form (Lebanese bank deposits vs. banknotes). **Circumvention reaction** (μ medium): movement toward alternative pathways with lower μ such as stablecoins, commodity proxies, or underground exchange (USDT in Argentina). Humans will circumvent access restrictions by any means available. Complete blockage is nearly impossible.

**State-dependence of μ.** μ is not a fixed value. A pathway with μ ≈ 0 in normal times can abruptly jump to μ → 1 during a crisis in a discontinuous manner. Money market funds in 2008 (instant redemption in normal times → redemption halt after Lehman), Russian bank deposits in 2022 (free withdrawal in normal times → frozen after sanctions). These sudden shifts constitute one of the core mechanisms of financial panics.

### The Narrative of Transition

The five states are not a linear sequence but **a set of possible states**. Not every cycle begins at state 1 — the Mesopotamian temple economy began as centralized (states 2–3), and China's DCEP begins at state 3. However, the principle that whenever the medium technology transitions, the preservation-coordination equilibrium resets is universal:

1. **Decentralized emergence** = the phase in which medium properties favor preservation force (natural scarcity of gold, mathematical scarcity of BTC)
2. **Capture by coordination force** = the phase in which coordination force finds technical gaps in the medium (verification cost → delegation, physical weight → custody delegation → fractional reserves, zero issuance cost → failure of restraint)
3. **Institutionalization** = full entrenchment of coordination force (fiat currency, tax linkage, issuance monopoly)
4. **Technological counter-offensive by preservation force** = when coordination force reaches an extreme, pressure from preservation force accumulates, and new medium technology realizes it. Bitcoin is currently the sole example (n=1), and this theory does not justify Bitcoin as a historical inevitability — it describes only the existence of the pressure. The specific form of its realization depends on technological contingency.
5. **Capture of access pathways** = if the medium itself cannot be changed, increase the μ of access pathways to circumvent (ETFs, exchanges, KYC, capital controls). Where the Song dynasty directly seized the "printing press," modern states seize the "post offices" (exchanges, ETFs). However, since this is digital, physical confiscation is impossible → effects are ultimately limited.

**Acceleration of time scales.** The 1→2→3 transition took centuries in the metallurgical era (gold coins), was compressed to decades to a century in the print era (banknotes), and further to decades in the electronic era. As medium technology accelerates the speed of information diffusion, the speed of transitions in ledger authority accelerates alongside it.

**Cross-layer feedback.** Layer 2 is subject to influence from higher layers. When fractional reserves emerge in Layer 3 (credit), issuance authority is in effect distributed to commercial banks; when bubbles form in Layer 4 (self-reference), new intermediaries (derivatives, structured finance) are inserted into access pathways. Layers are sequentially ordered in terms of emergence but operate simultaneously and with mutual feedback in structural terms.

### Equity Capital of the Issuance Authority Holder

Why a $200 billion loss by the Federal Reserve does not directly cause the dollar's value to fall: the Fed is the creator, not the acquirer → no acquisition cost → irrelevant to USC. However, this does not mean "nothing happens" — losses manifest as the suspension of remittances to the Treasury, which is in effect an expansion of the fiscal deficit. What does not happen in a visible place (currency value) happens in an invisible place (the fiscal channel). This is a canonical example of coordination force finding the path of least resistance.

**Dormant in normal times, activated in crisis.** Four conditions under which equity capital becomes relevant: political (independence compromised), perceptual (misinterpretation → self-fulfilling), structural (a fixed-rate issuer's equity capital is directly tied to issuance authority), **temporal** (if losses persist over the long run, market participants' expectations shift — the Fed's losses have continued from 2022 to the present, 4+ years). The higher the dependence, the more relevant.

### Preservation-Coordination Manifestations

Preservation force: issuance constraints arising from the physical/mathematical properties of the medium. Maintenance of distributed ledger authority. Coordination force: concentration of authority through the gaps in the medium. Manipulation of the μ of access pathways.

## 3.4 Layer 3: Temporal Extension and Credit — Bringing Future USC into the Present

In Layers 1 and 2, money mediates present value. In Layer 3, money begins to traverse time. Mechanisms develop for bringing "future money" into the present — loans, credit, interest, futures, insurance — and this is what generates the quantitative expansion of money. The core of this layer is not merely an observation but a principle: **credit is the present issuance of claims against costs expected to be incurred in the future. The issuance of such claims immediately dilutes USC (the denominator of money supply increases while the numerator of invested costs remains unchanged), and when the claims are honored (when the debtor actually invests costs), USC is retroactively restored. If the claims are not honored, the dilution is permanent.**

### 3.4.1 The Essence of Temporal Extension: Claims Against Costs Expected to Be Incurred in the Future

The essence of temporal extension is **the present issuance of monetary claims against costs expected to be incurred in the future**. When a farmer borrows seed grain and promises to repay after the harvest, this is a conversion of "future costs to be incurred (harvest labor)" into present money. This conversion requires two preconditions: (1) trust that the debtor will actually invest sunk costs in the future, and (2) institutional mechanisms that underwrite that trust. The former is grounded in the trust mechanisms of Layer 0 (the human condition), and the latter in the enforcement mechanisms of Layer 2 (the protocol).

The reason this matters from a USC perspective is that credit creation **increases the money supply without actual sunk cost investment**. The person who lent the seed grain invested labor to obtain that grain (sunk cost exists). However, the grain received by the farmer represents a state in which no labor has yet been invested — only a future promise exists. This gap is the principle underlying fractional reserve banking, and it is the root of USC dilution.

### 3.4.2 Credit Creation and Fractional Reserve Banking: The USC Amplification Mechanism

Fractional reserve banking is the most powerful mechanism of Layer 3. When a bank receives a deposit of 100 and lends out 90, the depositor believes they hold 100, while the borrower uses 90. Across the economy as a whole, the money supply increases from 100 to 190. As this process repeats across multiple stages, the money multiplier effect appears.

Restated in the USC framework: the person who originally deposited 100 invested 100 units of sunk cost — they hold 100 units of claims. However, because 190 units of money circulate through lending, **USC per unit of money falls to 100/190 ≈ 0.53.** This is the precise mechanism of USC dilution through credit creation. The additional 90 units of money exist in a state in which real sunk costs have not yet been invested — money that exists on the basis of a future promise alone.

The reason this does not pose a problem in normal times is that most debtors actually honor the promise by investing costs (through labor, business revenue, etc.) and repaying their loans. When the promised future cost investment is actually fulfilled, USC dilution is resolved. Crisis arises when this premise collapses — when debtors fail to invest the promised costs (default). At that moment, it is revealed that money created through credit existed without the backing of actual sunk costs, and this is the USC interpretation of credit crunches and bank runs.

Historically, this mechanism is directly linked to the "physical weight" problem described in Layer 2. The moment a goldsmith issued more receipts than the gold held in custody — the origin of fractional reserve banking — the delegation of custody opened the door to credit creation. The Amsterdam Bank (Wisselbank) in the seventeenth century began with 100% reserves but, by the late eighteenth century, had its effective reserve ratio decline due to secret loans to the Dutch East India Company. This is directly connected to the decline of the Dutch Empire discussed in Layer 6.

### 3.4.3 Interest Rates: The Temporal Premium on USC

Interest is not simply "the price of money." In the USC framework, interest is **compensation for the risk of temporal USC dilution**. The lender relinquishes the certain sunk costs of the present (money obtained through already-invested labor) and accepts an uncertain repayment in the future. Interest is compensation for this uncertainty — the default risk of the debtor, USC dilution through inflation, and opportunity costs.

This interpretation explains why interest rates are high in economies with high inflation. Because inflation accelerates the rate of USC dilution, lenders demand greater compensation. It also explains why Bitcoin has no intrinsic interest rate — because the 21-million-coin ceiling structurally blocks USC dilution through issuance, the risk of temporal USC dilution is itself structurally constrained.

From the perspective of preservation-coordination dynamics, the interest rate is an indicator of the equilibrium point between the two forces. In environments where preservation force is dominant (low inflation, stable USC), interest rates fall, and in environments where coordination force operates excessively (high inflation, rapid USC dilution), interest rates rise. The period in the 2020s when U.S. real interest rates were negative is interpreted as a state in which coordination force had reached an extreme — a structure that transfers the cost of USC dilution onto lenders.

#### The Dual Determination of Interest Rates

However, the observed interest rate is not the product of a single force. Two independent determination mechanisms operate simultaneously:

**(a) Market interest rate** = the compensation demanded by market participants for temporal USC dilution. Endogenously determined within Layer 3. The interest rate described above is this.

**(b) Ledger authority interest rate** = the rate directly set by the central bank in the exercise of its Layer 2 ledger authority. Exogenously determined within Layer 2.

The observed interest rate is the resultant of (a) and (b), and **there is no obligation for the two to coincide.** When (a) ≠ (b), the unpaid gap is realized through one of three pathways:

1. **Exchange rate pathway**: The USC of the relevant currency falls relative to other currencies. The Bank of Japan's maintenance of YCC (Yield Curve Control) for eight years resulted in the gap between the compensation demanded by the market and the rate set by the Bank of Japan being realized as a 35% decline in the yen.

2. **Asset price pathway**: The unpaid gap is absorbed into the prices of real assets such as real estate and equities. The mechanism by which suppressed interest rates generate asset bubbles is precisely this.

3. **Delayed explosion pathway**: When suppression is lifted, the accumulated unpaid gap is realized all at once. This is why policy pivots are accompanied by market shocks.

Interest rate suppression via ledger authority is a mechanism that **institutionally compels the overestimation** of the probability that future cost investments will be realized. It is not that markets err on their own — the accurate price signal itself is blocked.

This dual determination structure is isomorphic to Knut Wicksell's distinction between the natural rate and the market rate of interest. What USC adds to Wicksell's framework is the point that the divergence between the two need not be realized only as inflation but can also be realized as **liquidity evaporation**. The fact that in the 2008 financial crisis CPI remained intact while financial markets were frozen is the evidence — this phenomenon is difficult to explain within Wicksell's framework, but it is natural within the dual determination structure of USC.

### 3.4.4 The Temporal Chain of Monetary Value: A Revision of Mises' Regression Theorem

Ludwig von Mises' regression theorem holds that the present value of money is based on yesterday's purchasing power, which is based on the purchasing power of the day before, and tracing this back through time one arrives at the original "use value."

This theory revises that claim: it generalizes "use value" to "sunk cost." The terminus of the regression is not the intrinsic use value of an object but the sunk costs accumulated at the outset. This revision matters because Bitcoin possesses no "use value" in the traditional sense. Bitcoin cannot be drunk, eaten, or put to any industrial purpose. Yet the sunk costs of mining — electricity, hardware, time — are present. When the regression theorem is reinterpreted as "the temporal operation of USC," it applies to Bitcoin in the same way.

### 3.4.5 The Cantillon Effect: The Unequal Distribution of New Money Inflows

The most important consequence of temporal extension is the Cantillon effect. When new money flows into the system, those closest to the point of entry acquire money at the lowest USC, while those further away must acquire it at already-elevated prices.

The example of the Roman Empire illustrates this starkly. The emperor obtained denarrii with reduced silver content at USC ≈ 0. The Praetorian Guard received theirs directly from the emperor. The provincial farmer received his denarrii at already-elevated prices, after the emperor and the Guard had already spent theirs. This structure (emperor → Praetorian Guard → farmer) is precisely isomorphic to the modern central banking system (central bank → commercial banks → ordinary citizens), despite a temporal gap of two thousand years.

### 3.4.6 Inter-Civilizational Cantillon Effects: The International Operation of USC Inequality

A new concept identified in this research: the Cantillon effect operates not only domestically but also between civilizations.

The case of cowrie shells in West Africa constitutes decisive evidence. Europeans were able to obtain cowries in large quantities at low cost (low USC). These were introduced in large volumes into West Africa. While earning 500% profits, Europeans caused 1,000-fold inflation in West Africa. This is structurally identical to the influx of Spanish silver from the Americas, and also identical in structure to the effects of U.S. dollar quantitative easing on emerging economies today.

Restated in USC terms: **when money flows from regions of low USC toward regions of high USC, the USC of the receiving region is diluted.** This is the essence of the Cantillon effect, which operates identically whether domestically or between civilizations.

### 3.4.7 From Layer 3 to Layer 4: The Conditions for Transition

Credit in Layer 3 is the present issuance of claims against costs expected to be incurred in the future. So long as these claims remain sound — so long as there is a reasonable probability that the promised future cost investments will actually be fulfilled — the system remains within Layer 3.

The transition begins when **the probability of realizing the promised future cost investments begins to be systematically overestimated**. As a period of stability lengthens, participants' risk perception diminishes; diminished risk perception generates more optimistic future projections; more optimistic projections justify the advance appropriation of future costs on a larger scale. When this process repeats in a self-reinforcing manner, claims that are in reality impossible to fulfill at the promised scale begin to circulate as present money — this state is Layer 4.

This is the USC translation of Minsky's dictum that "stability is destabilizing."

Interest rate suppression through ledger authority (described in the dual determination of 3.4.3) is, as a mechanism that **institutionally compels** this overestimation, the most powerful catalyst for the Layer 3 → Layer 4 transition. When markets naturally underestimating risk is combined with institutions forcing that underestimation, the transition accelerates.

### 3.4.8 Regulatory Sunk Costs: An Extension of the USC Framework

The scope of USC application is not limited to traditional currencies. Even in assets allocated by governments at no cost — most notably carbon emissions permits — USC ≠ 0.

Carbon permits are allocated at zero acquisition cost, but the cost that holders must pay in order **not to use** those permits — the Marginal Abatement Cost (MAC) — functions as the functional equivalent of sunk costs. For a coal-fired power plant to hold emissions permits, it must reduce production, and that reduction in production is a real cost.

There are two reasons this interpretation is important. First, because MAC differs across holders, heterogeneous USC is formed, and this explains the price discovery inefficiency and high volatility of emissions markets — the noise in price signals is greater than in currencies with homogeneous USC. Second, the structure in which the government's changes to allocation volumes (coordination force) conflict with the value preservation based on MAC (preservation force) is precisely isomorphic to the preservation-coordination dynamics in currency.

## 3.5 Layer 4: Financial Self-Proliferation and Instability — The Divergence Between USC and Market Price

In Layer 3, money began to traverse time. In Layer 4, money **begins to multiply itself**. Credit begets credit, leverage begets leverage, financial products beget financial products. This layer reconstructs Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis (FIH) within the USC framework. The core principle of this layer is: **the divergence between market price and USC expands in a self-reinforcing manner through financial self-proliferation, and this expansion inevitably culminates in collapse.**

### 3.5.1 Minsky's Cycle Reconstructed in USC

Minsky's central insight — "Stability is destabilizing" — restated in USC terms reads as follows: during periods of stability in which market price is near USC, participants behave soundly, but precisely this stability promotes gradual risk-seeking behavior that pulls market prices above USC, which in turn generates more risk-seeking, ultimately resulting in collapse.

Minsky's three stages, defined in terms of USC, are as follows.

**Hedge Finance:** Participants' cash flows are sufficient to repay both principal and interest. USC interpretation: market price is near USC, and participants' actual sunk cost investments underpin market valuations. The monetary system is stable in this phase.

**Speculative Finance:** Cash flows can service interest but principal repayment depends on rollover. USC interpretation: market price substantially exceeds USC, and participants act on the basis of unrealized gains. Principal repayment depends on the sustained rise of market prices — that is, on the maintenance of the divergence between USC and market price.

**Ponzi Finance:** Cash flows cannot even service interest, with dependence solely on asset price appreciation. USC interpretation: MVRV has risen to extreme levels, with the divergence between market price and USC expanding in a self-reinforcing manner, and only the sunk cost inputs of new entrants sustain the system. As MVRV rises, the probability of collapse increases nonlinearly; the zone around 3.7 has been observed as the critical range across Bitcoin's four cycles. However, this figure is not a law but the present expression of a structural principle and may be adjusted by additional cycle data. The moment new entrants stop, collapse occurs.

The key to this transition lies in its **gradualism**. The transition from hedge to speculative, and from speculative to Ponzi, occurs naturally from within the system without any external shock. As the period of stability lengthens, participants become convinced that "this time is different," take on more leverage, and accept riskier financial products. This is the precise meaning of Minsky's "stability is destabilizing."

That the MVRV ratio is the quantitative measure of this cycle has already been described in 2.2.3. Bitcoin's halving schedule (described in Layer 2) generates a supply shock approximately every four years, which influences the timing of Minsky cycles.

### The USC Mechanism of "Stability Is Destabilizing"

Minsky's central insight is restated with precision in USC terms. During the **stable phase**, MVRV is in the range of 1.0–1.5 and lending is sound. **The beginning of the transition** comes when, as stability persists, risk perception changes, promoting increased leverage and driving MVRV above 1.5. During **acceleration**, once MVRV exceeds 2.5, unrealized gains accumulate on a large scale — a product not of actual sunk cost investment but of price appreciation — constituting a phantasmal expansion of wealth without underlying sunk costs ("the wealth effect"). **Collapse** follows the reverse cycle of price decline → collateral value decline → margin calls → forced selling, driving MVRV below 1.0. The speed of collapse is always faster than the ascent.

### Quantitative Mapping of Minsky's Three Stages to USC/MVRV

**Hedge Finance**: Cash flows can service all principal and interest. Future cost investment exceeds borrowing costs. MVRV 1.0–1.5.

**Speculative Finance**: Interest is serviceable, principal depends on refinancing. System stability depends on the functioning of financial markets. MVRV 1.5–2.5.

**Ponzi Finance**: Even interest is not serviceable; complete dependence on asset price appreciation. The divergence between underlying USC and market price is at maximum. MVRV > 3.7.

### Financial Innovation and the Layering of Leverage

Financial innovation is the process of stacking derivative value atop underlying USC. Mortgage (1st order) → MBS (2nd order) → CDO (3rd order) → CDS (4th order). When the USC of lower layers is diluted, upper layers collapse simultaneously. In the 2008 financial crisis, the sunk costs of the underlying asset (housing) were unchanged while derivative values had inflated by tens of multiples; a decline in the underlying price triggered the cascading collapse of the entire structure. This is the dramatic realization of the mechanism by which Layer 3 (credit amplification) transitions to Layer 4 (financial self-proliferation).

### Layer 4 Dynamics Across Bitcoin's Four Cycles

Bitcoin allows Layer 4 dynamics to be observed in pure form. In the early cycles (2011–2017), derivatives and leverage were limited, so only pure sunk cost accumulation and market psychology were at work. The common pattern: halving (Layer 2) → price rise → new entrants → self-reinforcing loop → rising MVRV → critical threshold (~3.7) reached → downward loop → convergence toward USC. The trend of MVRV cycle peaks declining (7.0 → 5.5 → 4.7 → 3.8) is evidence of market maturation. Since the 2021 cycle, the introduction of futures, options, and leveraged trading in earnest has made the dynamics increasingly complex. The 2024 spot ETF represents the process of layering traditional financial structures atop Bitcoin.

### 3.5.2 Leverage: The Amplifier of the Market Price–USC Divergence

Leverage is the core instrument of Layer 4. Purchasing assets with borrowed funds yields large market exposure from small sunk cost inputs. From the USC perspective, leverage is **a mechanism that amplifies the divergence between an individual's actual sunk costs and their market valuation**.

A participant who purchases 100 of assets with 10% equity has a real sunk cost of 10, yet creates 100 of demand in the market. Aggregated at the system level, this results in **the state in which total market capitalization greatly exceeds total sunk costs** — that is, a rising systemic MVRV.

Leverage amplifies gains during upswings but amplifies losses during downswings. A 10% decline in market price means a 100% loss for a 10x-leveraged participant. This triggers forced liquidations (margin calls), forced liquidations generate additional selling, and additional selling generates additional declines — a deleveraging spiral emerges. The speed of contagion in the 2008 global financial crisis was determined by this deleveraging spiral.

### 3.5.3 Financial Innovation and the Invisibility of USC

Another important characteristic of Layer 4 is that financial innovation makes USC increasingly difficult to measure. Derivatives, securitization, and shadow banking render the relationship between sunk costs and market prices complex and opaque.

The subprime mortgage crisis is the paradigmatic case. The original sunk cost (the borrower's repayment capacity) was clear. But as loans were securitized into MBS (Mortgage-Backed Securities), MBS were repackaged into CDOs (Collateralized Debt Obligations), and CDSs (Credit Default Swaps) were layered onto CDOs, the relationship between the original sunk cost (repayment capacity) and market valuations became entirely obscured. No one could know what USC actually was.

This is the paradox of financial innovation: financial innovation is justified as reducing transaction costs and distributing risk, yet simultaneously it reduces the traceability of USC and conceals the actual soundness of the system. Bitcoin's on-chain transparency — a structure in which all transactions are recorded and USC can be measured in real time — is the first technological solution in history to this problem.

### 3.5.4 The Arbitrariness of Bubble Objects

Another characteristic of Layer 4 is that even the selection of targets for bubbles is arbitrary. Tulip mania (the Netherlands), the South Sea Bubble (England), the dot-com bubble (the United States), the housing bubble (2008) — the object of speculation differs by era and context, but the mechanism (hedge → speculative → Ponzi) is identical. This is isomorphic to Layer 1's "adoption" — the first spark (the choice of currency) is arbitrary, and the object of the bubble (the target of speculation) is equally arbitrary.

## 3.6 Layer 5: Ecological Competition — When Multiple Currencies Coexist

Through Layer 4, the dynamics addressed were internal to a single currency — adoption, ledger authority, credit, self-reference. Layer 5 introduces the new degree of freedom of **the existence of exit options**. The fact that holders can move to another currency generates new dynamics of competition, substitution, coexistence, and differentiation. Layer 5 addresses the **horizontal coexistence** of currencies — the cross-section.

### Core Principle: Single-Currency Optimal Performance Is Impossible

Optimization for preservation force (fixed supply, volatility permitted) and optimization for coordination force (flexible issuance, price stability) are mutually incompatible — one cannot simultaneously fix and flexibly adjust the supply. Furthermore, because the criteria for fitness differ by niche, no single optimum exists. Therefore, the coexistence of multiple currencies is a logical necessity. Historically as well, there is no case of a single currency monopolizing all niches — even under the gold standard, gold, silver, copper, and banknotes coexisted.

In Layers 0–4, the preservation-coordination conflict operated within a single currency. In Layer 5, the same conflict **extends between currencies**. The inter-currency expression of preservation force: the force driving movement toward currencies with higher USC (dollarization, "searching for good money"). The inter-currency expression of coordination force: the state's force to maintain its own currency (legal tender laws, capital controls, designation of the currency for tax payment). The preservation-coordination tension that cannot be resolved within individual currencies is **functionally separated at the ecosystem level through inter-currency division of labor** — preservation-force currencies (Bitcoin, gold) and coordination-force currencies (fiat currencies) coexist.

### Determinants of Ecosystem Structure

Whether the monetary ecosystem trends toward winner-take-all or niche-specific coexistence is determined by three variables.

**First, the scope of network effects.** If more users mean greater utility across all purposes, the tendency toward winner-take-all is strong. If utility is confined to specific purposes, coexistence is possible. In the metallic currency era, network effects were universal (gold coins used for all transactions), so the winner-take-all tendency was strong. In the digital era, niche-specific network effects (BTC = storage, USDT = payments, ETH = DeFi) operate, making a coexistence structure natural.

**Second, multi-homing costs.** The cost of using multiple currencies simultaneously. In the metallic era, the physical costs of carrying multiple gold coins were high, so the winner-take-all tendency was strong. In the digital era, BTC, ETH, and USDT can all be held simultaneously in a single wallet, so the cost is nearly zero. Medium technology (Layer 2) determines multi-homing costs, which in turn constrain ecosystem structure.

**Third, political switching barriers.** Legal tender laws, capital controls, and the designation of specific currencies for tax payment form artificial lock-in. Even when the technological cost of multi-homing is zero, political barriers can block coexistence. Conversely, when barriers are removed (liberalization, regime collapse), rapid transitions occur.

### The Fitness of Currencies

What determines the survival and extinction of currencies in the ecosystem: **fitness = USC preservation capacity + accumulated sunk costs (switching costs)**. USC preservation capacity is how well the currency preserves the holder's acquisition cost, and the criteria differ by niche — in the value storage niche, supply limitation is essential; in the payments niche, price stability is essential.

High fitness does not always mean victory. As W. Brian Arthur's theory of increasing returns shows, a currency that achieves a slight early advantage and reaches lock-in cannot be displaced even if a "more fit" currency emerges. Bitcoin Cash (BCH) losing to Bitcoin (BTC) despite technical differences is the canonical case — lock-in through accumulated sunk costs dominated fitness. By contrast, Ethereum succeeded in coexisting with Bitcoin despite Bitcoin's lock-in by opening a new niche (smart contracts) — competing within an existing currency's niche leads to defeat by lock-in, but creating a new niche makes coexistence possible.

### Niche Structure

Niches in the monetary ecosystem are distributed along the preservation-coordination spectrum:

| Niche | Core Function | Fitness Criterion | Current Dominant |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Value storage | Maximize USC preservation | Supply limitation, censorship resistance, survival history | Bitcoin, Gold |
| Payments | Low volatility + fast settlement | Price stability, ease of access, settlement speed | Stablecoins, fiat currencies |
| Programmable finance | Implementation of complex logic | Developer ecosystem, composability | Ethereum |
| Coordination tool | Taxes, fiscal policy, public goods | Issuance flexibility, legal enforceability | National fiat currencies |
| Reserve | Central bank foreign reserves | Liquidity, stability, geopolitical safety | Dollar |
| Crisis response | Substitute when fiat fails | Real value, immediate accessibility | Soybeans, USDT, barter |

Niche boundaries are fluid. Bitcoin is attempting to enter the payments niche through the Lightning Network, and Ethereum is attempting to enter the value storage niche. **Niche invasion** is a core dynamic of the ecosystem. Competition within the same niche (intra-niche) has a strong winner-take-all tendency (BCH vs. BTC), while differentiation between different niches (inter-niche) enables coexistence (BTC vs. ETH vs. USDT). This is isomorphic to the competitive exclusion principle in biology.

### The Gresham-Thiers Integrated Cycle

Gresham's Law and Thiers' Law are not opposites but different manifestations of the same principle. People always act to maximize USC, but the institutional environment determines the pathway of expression:

**Stage 1 — Gresham Phase** (fixed exchange rate, artificial lock-in): With legally fixed exchange rates, currencies with high USC are hoarded and those with low USC are used. The disappearance of gold under nineteenth-century bimetallism is the paradigmatic case.

**Stage 2 — Transition Shock** (lock-in release): Artificial lock-in is broken by hyperinflation, regime collapse, or removal of capital controls.

**Stage 3 — Thiers Phase** (free exchange rate): Currencies with high USC, accumulated during the Gresham period, enter circulation. Currencies with low USC are rejected. In Russia in the 1990s, dollars kept under mattresses immediately entered circulation.

**Stage 4 — Hysteresis** (irreversibility): Once social and cognitive sunk costs accumulate in a new currency, it is difficult to revert. Bolivia maintained a high rate of dollarization even after hyperinflation stabilization (IMF, Guidotti & Rodriguez, 1992). Pound → dollar transition took 30+ years.

**Stage 5 — Re-monetization** (exceptional reversal): Re-monetization is possible if sunk costs in the old currency remain + tax enforcement operates. Russia (success): cognitive sunk costs in the ruble remained. Zimbabwe (failure): sunk costs completely dissipated.

These five stages constitute one cycle and are repeatable (Argentina: repeated for decades).

### Lock-in and Release

Lock-in is the fixation of a particular currency in a dominant position through network effects + accumulated sunk costs + political coercion. Three pathways to lock-in release:

**First, the triple sequential collapse of USC.** Economic USC collapse ("the value is falling") → social USC collapse (payment networks migrate to alternatives) → cognitive USC collapse ("this currency is dead"). Cognitive USC collapse is the last and most abrupt — in the final stage of hyperinflation, it proceeds within weeks. The difference in the speeds of the triple collapse explains the period during which "everyone knows the value is declining but continues to use the currency."

**Second, sudden stop.** The discontinuous transition from social to cognitive USC collapse analyzed by Guillermo Calvo. Self-reinforcing expectations ("others will exit too") operate. The dynamics of Layer 4 (self-reference amplification) operate in Layer 5 as inter-currency transitions.

**Third, the three conditions for platform overthrow.** From Jean Tirole's two-sided market theory: (a) when multi-homing becomes possible, (b) differentiated value proposition, (c) the incumbent platform's self-harm (inflation, asset freezes, access blockage). Digital technology weakens the technical foundations of lock-in (exchange in 3 seconds on a DEX), but what remains is political lock-in — even this, however, is a matter of raising access friction (Layer 2 μ) rather than physically blocking, and therefore can be circumvented.

### The Theorem of the Impossibility of Single-Currency Monopoly

There is no case in history of a single currency monopolizing all niches. The root cause: the incompatibility of preservation and coordination. Bitcoin's 21-million-coin ceiling maximizes preservation force but blocks coordination force at the source — if it were the sole currency, responses to war and pandemics would be impossible. Fiat currency's flexible issuance maximizes coordination force but structurally undermines preservation force. Bitcoin is likely to secure a dominant position in the value storage niche, but in coordination, payments, and programming niches, other currencies must inevitably coexist. In accordance with the neutrality principle of this theory, this is not a judgment that "Bitcoin fails."

## 3.7 Layer 6: Macro-Circulation — How Monetary Systems Rise and Fall

If Layer 5 addressed the **horizontal coexistence** of multiple currencies (the cross-section), Layer 6 adds the **temporal dimension** (the longitudinal section). How monetary systems are born, grow, decline, and transition through time. This is the temporal expression of Overarching Principle 2 (preservation-coordination dynamics).

### Core Principle: The Four-Phase Cycle

The preservation-coordination conflict cycles through four phases in time: **preservation dominance** (sound money, sunk cost accumulation) → **coordination infiltration** (monetary adjustment at the collective level begins) → **coordination dominance** (USC dilution, financial expansion, accumulation of complexity) → **collapse/reset** (USC crosses critical threshold, system resets). What repeats is the mechanism (the structure of the preservation-coordination conflict), not the events (specific wars, specific crises). The existence of the cycle is predictable, but the timing of turning points and their specific form are not.

This cycle **operates regardless of scale**. A single nation, an empire, a civilization — the same four phases repeat at different scales in a fractal structure.

### A. The Cycle of a Single Currency (National Scale)

The lifecycle of a currency issued by a single state. The four-phase cycle operates even without empire or reserve currency status.

Dynamics: fiscal pressure (war, regime transition, excessive welfare) → expansion of money issuance → USC dilution → inflation → when the critical threshold is crossed, hyperinflation → currency collapse → reset to new currency. This pattern is observed in all 29 cases of hyperinflation analyzed by Peter Bernholz.

**Argentina**: The same pattern has repeated on a cycle of several decades beginning in 1890, 1930, 2001, and 2018. Overlaps with the Gresham-Thiers cycle (Layer 5). **Medieval French livre**: The gradual debasement of silver content → crisis → reset repeated at least three times over 200 years (Philip IV, the Hundred Years' War, the Wars of Religion). **Zimbabwe** (2008): Complete dissipation of USC → abandonment of the domestic currency → adoption of the dollar. **Weimar Germany** (1923): War reparations → explosive issuance → hyperinflation → reset to the Rentenmark.

**The interpretation of stability periods.** The Byzantine solidus (700 years of stability) and the British pound gold standard (200 years of stability) are not the absence of the cycle but rather the prolonged continuation of Phase 1 (preservation dominance). The period during which the Byzantine economy was sufficiently large that coordination force needs could be met through taxation. When the conditions collapsed (Seljuk invasion of the eleventh century, territorial loss), it entered the cycle.

### B. The Cycle of a Hegemonic/Imperial Currency (Imperial Scale)

The same four phases as in A operate at global scale, with the additional dynamics unique to the formation of hierarchy, the public goods package, and peripheral dynamics.

#### B.1 The Self-Contradiction of the Reserve Currency (Triffin Dilemma)

The reserve currency nation must supply the world with money of low USC (provision of international liquidity = coordination force). However, this dilutes the USC of existing holders (erosion of preservation force). This is the Triffin Dilemma, the international version of the preservation-coordination conflict. This is an international extension of the three pathways for procuring public goods described in Overarching Principle 2 (taxation → inflation → government bonds). The reserve currency nation employs the third pathway (government bonds) on a global scale, and in the process, USC dilution is distributed not only to its domestic population but to all dollar holders worldwide. This is both the singular privilege and the singular curse of the reserve currency.

An examination of historical cases in detail confirms that all six reserve currencies declined through the same structural contradiction — the incompatible tension between supplying international liquidity (coordination force) and preserving the USC of existing holders (preservation force).

**① The Portuguese cruzado (15th century):** The monopoly over the spice trade led to a concentration of gold flowing into Lisbon. However, a significant portion of this gold was repatriated as payment for Asian spices, and the gold that remained domestically was consumed in luxury imports by the aristocracy and the royal court. As a result, domestic manufacturing contracted (an early form of the "Dutch Disease"), and once the monopoly over the spice trade was broken, the economic foundation for maintaining hegemony was destroyed. This is the earliest form of the reserve currency's self-contradiction — the supply of liquidity in international trade eroding the domestic production base.

**② The Spanish real (16th century):** Silver from the mines of Potosí and Zacatecas was imported in large quantities at low cost. The USC of this silver was extremely low — the cost of extraction through forced labor (the mita system) was a fraction of silver production costs in Europe. The result was the "Price Revolution" across Europe, with prices rising three to four times over 100 years. Paradoxically, Spain itself, the greatest beneficiary of silver, suffered the most severe damage — the influx of silver caused domestic prices to spike, destroying the price competitiveness of Spanish manufacturing, and the Habsburg wars (suppression of the Dutch Revolt, Ottoman wars, maintenance of Italian territories) far exceeded silver revenues, leading to three state bankruptcies (1557, 1575, 1596). This is an instance of the Cantillon effect from Layer 3 operating at international scale, and simultaneously the most dramatic expression of the self-contradiction of Layer 6.

**③ The Dutch guilder (17th century):** The Amsterdam Bank (Wisselbank, 1609) provided the first stable deposit currency in Europe, and the guilder became the standard for international settlement. However, as the costs of maintaining maritime hegemony (four Anglo-Dutch Wars, colonial defense) increased, the lending policy of the Amsterdam Bank was corrupted — initially it maintained 100% reserves, but in the late eighteenth century, secret loans to the Dutch East India Company increased and the effective reserve ratio declined. When the French invasion of 1795 revealed these secret loans, confidence in the guilder collapsed. This is a canonical case of the promise to maintain preservation force (100% reserves) being gradually eroded by the pressure of coordination force (war costs, East India Company bailout).

**④ The French livre/franc (18th–19th centuries):** The fiscal crisis of the Ancien Régime (interest expenditure exceeding 50% of the budget) was the immediate catalyst for the French Revolution. The assignat issued by the revolutionary government was backed by confiscated church lands, but was over-issued to cover war costs and fiscal deficits, and within five years had collapsed to 0.5% of face value. Napoleon stabilized the currency with the franc germinal, but the enormous costs of the Napoleonic Wars drained France's fiscal base. It was fiscal exhaustion rather than military defeat that was decisive in France losing financial hegemony to England.

**⑤ The British pound (19th–20th centuries):** The pound sterling under the gold standard embodied the maximization of preservation force — "a promise on the pound was a promise on gold." However, the costs of two World Wars destroyed this promise. Gold convertibility was effectively suspended to finance the First World War, and Churchill's forced return to gold at the old parity in 1925 exacted the price of export contraction and mass unemployment due to an overvalued exchange rate. The Second World War was decisive — the provision of war materiel from the United States under Lend-Lease was in effect a process of transferring sunk costs accumulated in the pound to the dollar. The world's central bank reserves of pounds, which were twice those of dollars in 1928, were reversed by the 1950s. This 30-year transition period demonstrates the scale of sunk cost transfer required for a reserve currency transition.

**⑥ The U.S. dollar (20th–21st centuries):** Under the Bretton Woods system (1944), the dollar was fixed to gold and other currencies to the dollar. This was the formal beginning of the Triffin Dilemma — supplying dollar liquidity for global economic growth (coordination force) continuously increased the ratio of dollar issuance to U.S. gold reserves, threatening the gold convertibility promise (preservation force). Nixon's 1971 suspension of gold convertibility was the inevitable consequence of this contradiction. The dollar was subsequently reorganized on the new trust foundation of the U.S. Treasury market, but the self-contradiction was not resolved — only its form changed. The quantitative easings (QE1–QE4) after 2008, the unlimited quantitative easing of 2020, and the national debt that has now surpassed $36 trillion are all the latest expressions of this self-contradiction.[^oh-dollar-geopolitics]

[^oh-dollar-geopolitics]: Taemin Oh analyzes the structural limits of post-Bretton Woods dollar hegemony in the context of U.S.-China rivalry, positioning Bitcoin as the emerging universal order of a "post-Bretton Woods" era. Oh Taemin, *Bitcoin, and the Geopolitics of the Dollar* (Geoin's Garden, 2023).

The pattern running through these six cases is clear. Reserve currency nations build trust initially through preservation force (sound money), but as the costs of maintaining hegemony (wars, colonies, alliances, international public goods) increase, they come to depend on coordination force (monetary expansion), which progressively erodes the initial preservation force promise, ultimately resulting in the loss of reserve currency status. Beginning with the promise of preservation force, ending with the abuse of coordination force.

#### The USC Lifecycle of Reserve Currencies — A Six-Stage Model

The universal lifecycle extracted from the six historical cases above is set out explicitly as follows:

**Stage 1 — Accumulation (material expansion):** The new hegemon rises on the strength of a real economy. The economic sunk cost of the currency is rapidly accumulated. Preservation force is dominant. The promise of sound money builds trust. (United States: 1870s–1945. Rome: the Republic through Augustus)

**Stage 2 — Public Goods Provision (establishment of hegemony):** Reserve currency status is established. International liquidity, safe assets, and security public goods are supplied. Social and cognitive sunk costs are accumulated ("the dollar is safe," "U.S. Treasuries are risk-free"). The coordination force of the reserve currency nation expands from the domestic to the global level. (United States: 1945–1971. Britain: 1815–1870)

**Stage 3 — The Financial Inflection Point:** Real growth slows and financial instruments begin to be used to maintain hegemony. The basis of USC shifts from economic sunk costs (labor, resources, production) to social and cognitive sunk costs (network inertia, beliefs). This transition is the passage from the material expansion phase to the financial expansion phase analyzed by Wallerstein-Braudel-Arrighi — and it is an **irreversible bifurcation point**. The GDP share of the financial sector rises sharply, and the offshoring of the real sector (deindustrialization) begins. (United States: 1971 Nixon Shock. Britain: 1890s onward)

**Stage 4 — Financial Expansion Phase (shift in USC basis):** Liquidity expansion, rising leverage, accumulation of financial complexity. Coordination force overwhelms preservation force. In the same currency, economic USC weakens while the inertia of social and cognitive USC sustains it. Minsky's cycle of Layer 4 (self-reference amplification) is applied to the reserve currency system as a whole. (United States: 1971–present. Britain: 1890–1914)

**Stage 5 — Friction Accumulation Phase:** Endogenous friction approaches structural limits. USC dilution continues but the system is superficially maintained through the inertia of social and cognitive sunk costs. However, frictions accumulate and mutually reinforce. Causal chain: Triffin dilution → Cantillon gap → political polarization → fiscal restraint impossible → debt acceleration → approaching the Ferguson Limit. Simultaneously, coordination force tolerance → need for larger interventions → approaching the Tainter Limit. When the two pathways converge, the Kindleberger Burden manifests — the will to supply is exhausted and the public goods package contracts.

**Stage 6 — Transition Phase (USC crossover):** The USC of the new currency approaches and crosses the USC of the old currency. The three categories of USC cross at different speeds: economic USC crosses first (U.S. GDP > U.K. GDP in the 1890s), social USC crosses later (dollar settlements > pound settlements in the 1940s), and cognitive USC crosses last. This difference in the crossing speeds of the three categories explains why reserve currency transitions take 30+ years. During the crossover period, a "co-reserve currency" regime exists (Eichengreen: pound-dollar co-reserve in the 1920s–1930s). During the transition, a last-resort lender vacuum (Kindleberger) occurs, and crisis frequency peaks (Reinhart-Rogoff: 1920s–1940s). The average lifespan of a reserve currency is roughly 80–120 years, but the dispersion is wide — the average is structural and the dispersion reflects historical contingency (policy choices, external shocks, technological change).

#### B.2 Formation of the Hierarchy

For a reserve currency to be established, three foundations must form a self-reinforcing loop. **Material foundation** (economic and military superiority — all reserve currency nations were simultaneously military hegemons), **public goods package** (international liquidity, safe assets, lender of last resort, security), **network effects** (more users → greater utility → more users). The relationship between monetary hierarchy and material hierarchy is co-evolution — neither simple reflection nor construction, but a feedback loop. The empire's public goods package sustains the monetary system, and the monetary system strengthens the empire's procurement capacity. Synchronization within ±20 years confirmed in 7 of 11 empire-currency pairs.

**The realities that prevent imperial currency from becoming a perpetual engine.** These frictions are classified into three categories: **(1) endogenous frictions** — arising from within the system and connected in causal chains (coordination force tolerance, Cantillon gap, generational turnover, Triffin dilution), **(2) structural limits** — manifesting when critical thresholds are reached (the Ferguson Limit, the Tainter Limit, the Kindleberger Burden), **(3) exogenous challenges** — the accumulation of USC by challenger currencies and the blowback from the weaponization of access rights. These are described below in order: structural limits, endogenous frictions, and exogenous challenges.

- **The Ferguson Limit [structural limit]:** The principle derived by Niall Ferguson — the moment debt interest expenditure exceeds defense spending, the empire begins to lose the capacity to maintain its public goods package (military protection, legal order, infrastructure). This limit is not merely a fiscal indicator but signifies the inversion of the raison d'être of the empire — the empire's legitimacy rests on providing a public goods package, but the structure in which past costs (interest) consume future public goods (defense) constitutes a fundamental inversion.
    - **Habsburg Spain:** Despite the enormous silver revenues from Potosí, the war costs of the Habsburg dynasty (suppression of the Dutch Revolt, Ottoman wars, maintenance of Italian territories) overwhelmingly exceeded silver income. Beginning with the first state bankruptcy in 1557, this was repeated in 1575 and 1596. The structure of paying interest to Genoese and Dutch financiers while maintaining armies — past war costs constraining present war capacity — is the canonical manifestation of the Ferguson Limit.
    - **Ancien Régime France:** In 1788, interest expenditure exceeded 50% of total royal revenues. The combined effect of the Seven Years' War (support for American independence), aristocratic tax exemptions, and resultant revenue shortfalls was the cause. The state attempted to overcome the crisis by issuing the assignat, but this merely converted the crisis into a different form — inflation — the canonical trajectory of the self-contradiction.
    - **The Ottoman Empire:** Referred to as "the sick man of Europe" in the late nineteenth century, structural fiscal deficits were financed with foreign debt (borrowed from British and French bankers). In 1875, the world's first sovereign debt moratorium.
    - **The British Empire:** The national debt/GDP ratio surpassed 130% at the end of the First World War. The Second World War drove it to 250%. As a result, the empire's most important assets (1.4 billion dollars of dollar-denominated assets) were transferred to the United States under Lend-Lease (1941), and after the war even the military capacity to maintain the colonies was lost. The final stage of the Ferguson Limit: beyond the stage at which interest costs erode defense capabilities, the stage at which the empire's own assets are transferred to creditors.
    - **The United States:** In 2024, federal net interest expenditure of $881B exceeded the defense budget of $850B for the first time. This is the breach of the Ferguson Limit. However, the United States holds conditions decisively different from previous empires — since the dollar is the reserve currency, government bonds can be issued in domestic currency, which delays the timeline of crisis. But the structural mechanism is the same.
- **The Self-Destruction of Coordination Force [endogenous friction]:** When every crisis is responded to with USC dilution (monetary expansion, government bond issuance), the next crisis requires greater dilution — because the previous dilution becomes the new baseline. This is isomorphic to drug tolerance — the same effect requires ever-larger doses, while side effects (inflation, asset bubbles, USC dilution) accumulate. Looking at actual scale: for the United States, QE1 in 2008 was $600B; QE unlimited in 2020 exceeded $3T; and the fiscal deficit in 2020–2021 exceeded 15% of GDP annually. The scale of each prior crisis response becomes the "baseline" for the next crisis, and the scale of each round of intervention grows geometrically. The structural problem of this self-destructive feedback loop is that the efficacy of coordination force gradually diminishes while the side effects accumulate. The same mechanism operated with the accelerating debasement of silver in the late Roman denarius — early on, a 10% reduction in silver content was sufficient to cover finances, but later 50%, 80%, and 96% debasement became necessary. Adjustment is a self-destructive feedback loop that weakens coordination force itself.
- **Wealth Inequality and Internal Conflict [endogenous friction — Cantillon gap]:** Through the Cantillon effect (Layer 3), when new money enters the system, financial institutions and asset holders benefit first, while wage earners face the already-elevated prices last. This structurally expands the wealth gap, and this expansion generates political polarization, and polarization makes political consensus on fiscal restraint impossible, accelerating debt accumulation. - **The Rise of External Challengers [exogenous challenge]:** While the empire is exhausted internally (rising costs, wealth inequality, political deadlock), competitors replicate and improve upon its technology and institutions at lower cost and overtake it. This is a paradoxical effect created by the empire's own public goods package — challengers grow within the stable environment (legal order, military protection, trade infrastructure) provided by the empire and ultimately replace it. Historical pattern: England grew within the Dutch maritime trade network and replaced the Netherlands; the United States and Germany industrialized within the British free trade system and replaced Britain. - **Generational Turnover [endogenous friction]:** When the generation that directly experienced the crisis withdraws from decision-making power, similar mistakes recur approximately 75 years later. Ray Dalio's Big Cycle framework models the rise and fall of empires in six stages: Stage 1 (emergence of a new order and consolidation) → Stage 2 (construction of a resource allocation system) → Stage 3 (peace and prosperity) → Stage 4 (excess and waste) → Stage 5 (financial difficulties and internal conflict) → Stage 6 (civil war or revolution). The length of this cycle is approximately 75 years — the time it takes for the generation that directly experienced a crisis to disappear. The core insight: the generation that experienced the crisis leads fiscal restraint and structural reform, but when that generation withdraws from decision-making power, institutional memory is lost and the same mistakes are repeated. Restated in USC terms: the crisis-experienced generation values preservation force because it has visceral memory of the consequences of USC dilution, but the generation without that memory underestimates the costs of coordination force and again repeats USC dilution. In writings and interviews from late 2025 and early 2026, Dalio diagnosed the United States as being in late Stage 5, on the verge of entering Stage 6 — on the basis of: debt/GDP ratios at historically elevated levels (122%+), political polarization at its highest since the 1860s, the wealth gap at its greatest since the 1920s, and the breach of the Ferguson Limit. From the perspective of this theory, Dalio's Big Cycle is interpreted as a compound cycle in which preservation-coordination oscillation and generational turnover are superimposed. From the Great Depression of 1929, approximately 75 years → the 2008 financial crisis. From the establishment of the Bretton Woods system in 1945, approximately 75 years → the challenges to dollar hegemony in the 2020s. Generational forgetting produces the same mistakes.
- **Diminishing Returns on Complexity [structural limit — Tainter Limit]:** Joseph Tainter's *The Collapse of Complex Societies* (1988): societies increase complexity to solve problems (bureaucracy, regulation, financial products). Initially, increased complexity yields high returns, but as marginal returns diminish, complexity itself eventually becomes a cost. ## 4.1 Cowrie Shells: The Complete Arc from Adoption to Displacement

Cowrie shells in West Africa functioned as money for approximately six hundred years, from the fourteenth century to the mid-twentieth century. This case is a rare instance in which one can observe, within a single narrative, the adoption of money (Layer 1), USC dilution by external shock (Layer 3), an attempt by colonial power to seize ledger authority (Layer 2), competition and coexistence between currencies (Layer 5), and imperial monetary hierarchy (Layer 6).

### Layer 1 — Why Were Cowries Chosen?

An analysis of the reasons why cowrie shells (*Cypraea moneta*) were adopted as money in West Africa, using the theory's three determining factors:

**Observability of acquisition cost**: Cowries were harvested in the Maldive Islands and introduced into West Africa via Indian Ocean trade routes. The trade distance itself — spanning thousands of kilometers — served as evidence of acquisition cost. To obtain a handful of cowries, one had to participate in a long-distance trade network, and the cost of that participation was observable to the community.

**Focal point**: Cowrie shells possessed religious and aesthetic value in West Africa. They were used in rituals, employed as ornamentation, and served as expressions of status. This cultural salience functioned as the trigger for the social convergence around "this is worth valuing."

**Contextual availability**: A steady supply arrived in West Africa through the Indian Ocean–Arabia–Sahara trade routes, and their naturally uniform size made counting easy. Because supply was geographically constrained (mass harvesting was possible only in the Maldives), the structural accumulation of USC was inherently possible.

### Layer 3 — The Great Cowrie Inflation (Inter-Civilizational Cantillon Effect)

In the sixteenth century, the Portuguese reached the cowrie's place of origin (the Maldives) directly. Thereafter, Europeans were able to procure cowries in bulk at a fraction of West Africans' acquisition cost. European cowrie acquisition USC ≪ West African cowrie acquisition USC.

The result: **16 billion shells were introduced into West Africa over several centuries.** The price of one enslaved person soared from 8,000 cowries (1724) to 80,000 (1748) to 192,000 (1773). This is described using the word "inflation," but the precise account within the USC framework is: **externally supplied money with low USC flooded in, diluting the sunk cost per monetary unit held by existing holders.**

This is the historical archetype of the "inter-civilizational Cantillon effect" described in Layer 3. When money flows from the lower-USC side (Europeans) to the higher-USC side (West Africans), the USC of the higher side is diluted. This is structurally isomorphic to the effect that modern quantitative easing has on emerging-market economies.

### Layer 2 — The Coercion and Failure of Colonial Ledger Authority

In the late nineteenth century, the French colonial government attempted to replace cowries with the franc. **It failed for decades.** The reasons:

- The **social and cognitive sunk cost** accumulated in cowries (centuries of transaction practice, cultural meaning, the unit of everyday economic life) could not be replaced by mere decree.
- **Constraints of medium technology**: European coins could not provide denominations as small as cowries. There was no physical means to replace cowries in small-denomination everyday transactions. This is a direct empirical demonstration of Layer 2 (the technical properties of the medium constrain the channels through which conflict is expressed).
- **Tax coercion alone was insufficient**: Taxes were enforced in francs, but the everyday economy continued to operate in cowries. This is a case in which Layer 2 ledger authority (taxation = control of access rights) could not immediately displace the USC accumulated in Layer 1.

### Layer 5 — Ecological Competition: Coexistence of Cowries and the Franc

In the end, cowries and the franc **coexisted for decades.** This is a case in which Gresham's Law from Layer 5 was at work:

- The franc (demand driven by state coercion) was used for tax payments and official transactions
- The cowrie (inertia from existing USC) persisted in everyday small-denomination transactions and the informal economy

The two currencies occupied distinct **niches**. The franc occupied the "coordination tool" niche (taxes, formal economy), while the cowrie held the "everyday payment" niche. It is also a historical demonstration of the "no single currency can achieve total monopoly" theorem of Layer 5.

The point at which cowries finally ceased to function as money was the **1960s** — nearly a century after the onset of colonization. This reveals the sequential collapse speed of triple USC: economic USC (purchasing power) had already been eroded in the nineteenth century by the great cowrie inflation, but social USC (transaction practices) and cognitive USC (cultural meaning) held for another century.

### Layer 6 — Imperial Monetary Hierarchy

The displacement of the cowrie was not an isolated event but part of **France's establishment of a monetary hierarchy**. The origins of the CFA franc system (1945–present) lie here. The "extreme form of colonial monetary hierarchy" described in Layer 6 is the ultimate result of the cowrie displacement — a structure in which the authority to issue rests in Paris and access authority is controlled by France.

### What This Case Reveals About the Theory

1. **The necessity of multi-layer analysis**: A single causal chain — "Europeans imported cowries cheaply → inflation → displacement" — cannot explain why replacement took one hundred years, why cowries survived to the end in small-denomination transactions, or why the colonial government failed for decades. Only the simultaneous operation of five layers explains the full narrative.
2. **The sequential collapse speed of triple USC**: economic (fast) → social (slow) → cognitive (slowest). This difference in speed produced a century of coexistence.
3. **The role of medium properties**: The fact that a physically "superior" coin could not replace cowries for decades is direct evidence for Core Claim 1 ("USC, not medium properties, determines value"). At the same time, the coin's inability to provide cowries' small denominations is evidence for Layer 2 ("medium technology imposes constraints"). This shows that the two claims are not contradictory but capture different aspects.

## 4.2 The Roman Denarius: 254 Years of USC Dilution

The Roman denarius (AD 1–254) is the most thoroughly documented case of long-term USC dilution in monetary history. Its silver content was gradually diluted over 254 years from 3.9 g to 0.15 g (a 96% reduction), culminating in replacement by Constantine's solidus. This case cuts across Layers 2 (ledger authority), 3 (Cantillon effect), and 6 (the four phases of the macro-cycle), and provides the most dramatic illustration of the non-linearity of USC dilution.

### Layer 2 — The State Monopoly on Ledger Authority

In the Roman Empire, the right of coinage (issuance authority) was monopolized by the emperor. This is the "seizure of coordination power" state described in Layer 2 — although the physical properties of the medium (metal's natural scarcity) were favorable to the preservation of value, the monopoly on issuance authority opened the sole point of entry for coordination power. By reducing silver content, the emperor could **mint more coins from the same quantity of silver**.

### Layer 3 — The Cantillon Effect Operating over 254 Years

Newly minted debased denarii (with low USC) reached the emperor first. The emperor used them to pay the Praetorian Guard, and the Guard spent them in the marketplace. The provincial farmer received the debased coins last, after prices had already risen. "Emperor → Praetorian Guard → farmer" is precisely isomorphic to "central bank → commercial banks → ordinary citizens," despite a gap of two thousand years.

### Layer 6 — The Complete Cycle of the Four-Phase Circulation

The denarius's 254 years display the four-phase cycle of Layer 6 in complete form:

1. **Preservation dominance** (AD 1–c. 100): Augustus to Trajan. Silver content maintained at near-original levels (3.9 g → 3.4 g, a reduction of only c. 13%). The era of imperial expansion. Revenue from conquests and war spoils was sufficient to reduce the need for monetary dilution.
2. **Coordination penetration** (c. 100–200): Hadrian to Septimius Severus. Silver content 3.4 g → 1.5 g. Rising costs of imperial defense, border wars, and plague. Fiscal pressure drove gradual dilution.
3. **Coordination dominance** (c. 200–254): Caracalla to Gallienus. Silver content 1.5 g → 0.15 g. Civil wars of the Soldier Emperor era, external invasions, and fiscal collapse forced accelerating dilution.
4. **Collapse/Reset**: Diocletian's attempted reforms → final reset via Constantine's solidus (new gold coin standard).

### The Non-Linearity of USC Dilution

The most noteworthy point: the silver content was reduced by a factor of 25 (3.9 g → 0.15 g), yet prices rose approximately 200-fold. The linear model MV=PQ can predict only a 25-fold price increase. Where does the 8-fold discrepancy come from?

This theory's answer: **non-linear amplification produced by the sequential collapse of triple USC.** When the dilution of economic USC (silver content) crosses a threshold, social USC (trust in the payment network and transaction practices) collapses in a chain reaction, and cognitive USC (the cross-generational belief that "the denarius is valuable") collapses last. Each stage of collapse accelerates the next, generating non-linear amplification. This is a phenomenon that Friedman's quantity theory of money (MV=PQ) cannot explain, and it is the historical evidence for Incompatible Prediction 4 ("the price response to an identical change in the money supply varies qualitatively with the distribution of USC").

# Section 5: Predictions and Applications

## 5.1 Incompatible Predictions (Scientific Falsifiability)

For this theory to claim the status of a scientific theory, it must present predictions that are **incompatible** with existing theories and must be falsifiable (Popper). The following are four predictions that this theory makes that are incompatible with existing mainstream theories:

1. **Money introduced solely by state declaration (without spontaneous adoption) is structurally vulnerable in the long run** (vs. Chartalism). Chartalism holds that state declaration is the source of monetary value, but this theory predicts that money introduced by state declaration alone, without organic accumulation of USC, is structurally weak. El Salvador's failed legal-tender adoption of Bitcoin is consistent with this prediction. However, where residual sunk cost remains, re-monetization through tax coercion is possible (the Russian ruble case).

2. **Autonomous value through USC accumulation alone is possible without non-monetary commodity value** (vs. Mises's regression theorem). The regression theorem holds that the value of money must originate from an initial "commodity use value," but this theory predicts that sunk cost accumulation alone can substitute for use value. Bitcoin's fourteen-year empirical record supports this prediction — it cannot be drunk or eaten, yet sunk cost has accumulated.

3. **A currency that is unstable in value but has thick USC survives** (vs. Hayek). Hayek's competitive money theory holds that value stability is the core competitive advantage of money, but this theory predicts that the thickness of USC (accumulated sunk cost) is more important to survival than price stability. Bitcoin has survived four drawdowns of more than 80% — it is extremely unstable in value, but it survives because its USC is thick.

4. **Identical changes in the money supply produce different price responses depending on the distribution of USC** (vs. Friedman's quantity theory of money). Friedman holds that a change in M (money supply) is reflected proportionally in P (price level), but this theory predicts that even an identical increase in the money supply will produce qualitatively different price responses depending on the point of entry (Cantillon effect) and the USC distribution of existing holders.

The two foundational premises of this theory — the ontological claim that money is a ledger phenomenon (Section 2.1, Layer 2) and the governing principle that accumulated sunk cost determines monetary value (Section 2.2, Governing Principle 1) — have direct precursors in Oh Tae-min's monetary theory of Bitcoin. Citations have been provided at each relevant point in footnotes; nevertheless, it is necessary to gather in one place the full precursor work underlying both premises.

**Ledger ontology.** In the second lecture, "Money is a Ledger," of his EBS Class-e lecture series *Bitcoin: The One Thing I Don't Know* (2022), and in *The Great Bitcoin* (2024), Oh Tae-min advanced the following proposition: the essence of money is not its material substrate — gold, paper, or electronic circuitry — but the ledger itself that records "who holds how much"; the material is merely a historically contingent medium for physically implementing that ledger. The stone money of Yap (rai, including specimens resting on the seabed), gold transfers recorded as ledger entries in the vaults of the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, and the circulation of the Swiss dinar in Iraqi Kurdistan are all offered as inductive evidence. This framework resolves phenomena that neither metallism nor chartalism can adequately explain. Layer 2 (Ledger Authority) of the present theory adopts this ontology as its starting point, then extends it into a dynamic dimension that analyzes how the centralization, dispersal, and redistribution of ledger-management authority constrains the pathways through which preservation–coordination conflicts are expressed.

**Acquisition-cost hypothesis.** Under the proposition that "how money is acquired matters more than how it is used," Oh Tae-min designed the Bitmobick (BTCMobick) social experiment (2023–). Bitmobick was airdropped exclusively to participants who expended physical effort in nationwide hiking events; the experiment is a natural experiment verifying whether the sunk cost formed in the acquisition process itself operates as a mechanism sustaining holding psychology and price levels. This hypothesis shares the same intuitive core as Governing Principle 1 of the present theory (collective sunk cost). Oh Tae-min's body of work spans more than ten volumes, beginning with *Bitcoin Was Strong* (2014) and continuing through *Smart Contract: The Trust Revolution* (2018), *Bitcoin: Genealogy of Wisdom* (2020), *Bitcoin and the Geopolitics of the Dollar* (2023), *The Great Bitcoin* (2024), and *21 Million* (2025); in his capacity as adjunct professor in the Bitcoin and Monetary Philosophy program at Hanyang University, he has formed the institutional foundation of Bitcoin monetary theory in the Korean-language intellectual sphere.

**Differentiation from precursor work.** The present theory extends Oh Tae-min's ontology in three directions. First, **quantification**: whereas Oh Tae-min's acquisition-cost thesis remains at the level of qualitative observation, this theory defines Unit Sunk Cost (USC) as "total acquisition cost of all current holders ÷ total monetary supply," converting it into a figure that can be computed from on-chain data and historical records. Second, **dynamics**: whereas Oh Tae-min's analysis is confined to explaining value *formation*, this theory measures the divergence between market price and USC via the MVRV indicator, derives a nonlinear threshold (hits all four cycle tops at MVRV 3.7 with zero false positives across Bitcoin's four market cycles), and thereby presents a predictive structure for value *dynamics*. Third, **universalization**: whereas Oh Tae-min's analysis concentrates on Bitcoin as a single case, this theory encompasses the monetary histories of the Roman denarius, cowrie shells, Spanish silver, the British pound, and the US dollar — five civilizations, four continents, 5,000 years — within a single unified framework.
